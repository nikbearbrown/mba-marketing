# Chapter 20 — Distribution: Delivering Customer Value

*The Place P. Whatever else the firm does to its product, price, and promotion, the customer can't buy what they can't access.*

Here is a product problem that has nothing to do with the product.

Outstanding Foods makes PigOut — a vegan, bacon-flavored chip. The product works: vegans who want the flavor of bacon, and non-vegans curious about the category, are both plausible customers. The product passes Whole Foods' strict ingredient standards. The founders, Dave Anderson and Bill Glaser, want to reach both audiences at scale.

Their problem is not the chip. It's that "vegan snack buyers" and "mainstream snack buyers" don't shop in the same stores. Whole Foods captures one audience; mainstream grocery chains capture the other. To reach both, Outstanding Foods needed a broker network that could place the product in both channel types simultaneously — natural grocers and conventional supermarkets running in parallel.

The distribution problem had to be solved before the marketing could work. That is always the case. A product that's perfectly designed, fairly priced, and brilliantly promoted but unavailable when the customer wants to buy might as well not exist. Distribution is the operational layer that makes the rest of marketing real.

---

## What Marketing Channels Are, and Why Intermediaries Exist

A **marketing channel** is the system of people, organizations, and activities that moves goods from producers to consumers. It's not just shipping. It's the whole sequence of transactions, relationships, and physical movements through which ownership of goods transfers — from manufacturer to wholesaler to retailer to customer, or in some direct path from manufacturer straight to customer.

The simplest channel is **direct**: manufacturer to consumer, no intermediaries. A local pizza shop selling to walk-in customers. A manufacturer's website shipping directly to buyers.

The most common channels for consumer goods are **indirect**: manufacturer through one or more intermediaries to consumer. That same pizza shop, once it decides to sell frozen pizzas through grocery stores, has added an indirect channel. Between the manufacturer and the grocery-store shelf sit a set of intermediaries — wholesalers who aggregate from multiple manufacturers and sell to retailers, retailers who stock and sell to consumers, brokers who match sellers to buyers without taking ownership themselves.

The persistent temptation is to see intermediaries as pure cost — margin takers whose elimination would lower prices or increase profits. Sometimes this is true. Usually it isn't. The intermediary exists because they can perform the distribution function more cheaply than the manufacturer can at the same scale. A chip manufacturer that tried to place product in every grocery store in the country without a broker network would need a field sales force, account management infrastructure, and logistics capability that would cost far more than the broker's margin. The intermediary's margin buys services the manufacturer doesn't have to build.

The intermediaries vary by role. **Wholesalers** buy in bulk from manufacturers and sell in smaller quantities to retailers — aggregating supply and breaking it into distributable parcels. **Retailers** sell to end consumers and provide the physical or digital shelf space where purchase happens. **Brokers** connect buyers and sellers without taking ownership of the goods. **Agents** represent manufacturers to the channel, sometimes exclusively, sometimes across multiple non-competing products.

Each adds value. Each adds margin requirements that accumulate into the final price.

---

## Three Coverage Strategies

Once a firm has decided on a channel structure, it faces a second decision: how broadly to distribute within that structure. Three strategies exist, and the choice is tightly coupled to the product type and brand positioning.

**Intensive distribution** places the product in every possible intermediary — every grocery store, every convenience store, every vending machine. The goal is ubiquity. The customer should be able to find the product wherever they are, at whatever moment the purchase impulse strikes. Coca-Cola uses intensive distribution because it's a convenience product (Chapter 12): customers won't search for it, won't wait for it, and will substitute immediately if it's unavailable. Intensity is the strategy.

**Selective distribution** places the product in a carefully chosen subset of intermediaries — not everywhere, but in the outlets where the target customers actually shop. Many consumer electronics and mid-tier apparel brands use selective distribution. The retailer is chosen for quality, service capability, and alignment with the brand's positioning. The coverage is broad enough to reach the target segment; narrow enough to maintain some brand control over the purchase environment.

**Exclusive distribution** places the product in a single intermediary per territory or a very small set of premium partners. Luxury watches, high-end automobiles, certain professional equipment. The exclusivity serves the brand positioning directly: the scarcity of access is part of the product's meaning. A Patek Philippe that was available at every drugstore counter would no longer be a Patek Philippe in any meaningful sense.

The coupling rule: product type constrains the choice. Convenience products require intensive distribution — scarcity defeats the purchase. Specialty products allow exclusive distribution — scarcity serves the brand. Shopping products typically land on selective. A premium brand attempting intensive distribution produces the same problem as a specialty product on the wrong shelf: the distribution signal contradicts the product signal, and the customer is confused.

## Channel System Types

Channels also vary in how tightly they're coordinated. Four types:

A **vertical marketing system (VMS)** treats manufacturer, wholesaler, and retailer as a unified system — either through common ownership, contractual arrangement, or administered coordination. McDonald's franchisor-franchisee structure is a contractual VMS: the franchisor sets product standards, marketing, and operations; the franchisee owns and operates the unit. The system functions as a coordinated chain even though ownership is distributed.

A **horizontal marketing system** has two or more firms at the same channel level cooperating to reach customers neither could reach alone. The bank inside a grocery store is a horizontal partnership: the bank gets foot traffic it didn't generate; the grocery store offers a service it doesn't want to run.

A **multichannel marketing system** uses multiple distinct channels to reach different segments — a brand selling through its own retail stores, its website, and Amazon is multichannel. Each channel may serve a different segment or a different purchasing occasion.

An **omnichannel marketing system** is multichannel with integration. The customer experience flows seamlessly across channels: start a purchase on the phone, complete it in the store, return it through either. The inventory, customer data, and service history are unified across the channels. Most modern retailers are nominally multichannel; the ones that have done the harder work of integration are genuinely omnichannel.

The difference matters because customers don't stay in one channel. They research on a website, try in a store, purchase through an app, and return through whatever channel is convenient. A multichannel firm handles each channel independently; an omnichannel firm handles the customer's journey across all of them.

## Supply Chains: Everything Before the Customer Sees It

The channel gets the product to the customer. The supply chain gets the product to the channel. The two are distinct systems with different management challenges.

**Supply chain management (SCM)** coordinates all the members and activities from raw material procurement through finished-goods delivery — purchasing, operations, logistics, resource management, and information workflow. It is the invisible infrastructure that makes channel delivery possible.

Dave's Killer Bread illustrates how supply chain decisions are marketing decisions. The brand's promise — the best bread you've ever tasted, power-packed with organic whole grain nutrition — begins with selecting certified organic suppliers capable of consistently delivering high-quality grains. The supply chain has to deliver on what the marketing claims. Without aligned suppliers, the ingredient claim breaks. Without reliable logistics, the distribution promise breaks. The supply chain isn't behind the marketing; it is part of the marketing.

**Logistics** is the piece of SCM most visible in the final customer experience: warehousing, inventory management, and transportation.

**Warehousing** — stocking, receiving, picking, packing, shipping — is the hub where supply chain meets channel. Some warehousing requires temperature control; all of it requires inventory accuracy. A warehouse that ships the wrong product or ships late breaks the channel promise downstream.

**Inventory management** determines stock levels, reorder points, and turnover rates. Too much inventory ties up capital; too little produces stockouts. The discipline is calibrating between them in a demand environment that isn't perfectly predictable.

**Transportation** moves products between locations — truck, rail, ship, air, pipeline. Each mode makes different trade-offs: truck is flexible and fast but expensive per unit over long distances; rail is slow and geographically constrained but cheap per unit in bulk; air is fastest and most expensive; ship is cheapest per unit at the scale of global trade but slowest and geographically limited. Most modern supply chains combine modes at different stages.

**Third-party logistics (3PL) providers** handle warehousing, fulfillment, and transportation as a service. Outsourcing to a 3PL allows firms to scale distribution without building in-house infrastructure — a critical capability for brands that are growing faster than their operations can absorb.

The central trade-off in logistics is cost versus speed. Amazon Prime normalized two-day delivery and shifted consumer expectations in ways that have cascaded across every retail category. Faster shipping requires more frequent transportation runs, more distributed warehouse locations, more expensive last-mile delivery infrastructure. Every firm operating in consumer distribution is navigating the tension between what customers now expect and what the unit economics of their specific product can actually support.

## Sustainability in Distribution

Distribution decisions have disproportionate environmental impact — more, arguably, than product decisions — because logistics is carbon-intensive and packaging is waste-intensive at scale.

**Carbon footprint versus speed** is the direct tension. The expectation of fast delivery creates more frequent, less efficient shipping runs. Some firms now offer customers a slower-but-greener shipping option at lower cost or with an environmental commitment attached. Consolidating orders into fewer, fuller shipments reduces per-unit carbon substantially. Customer willingness to choose slower delivery for environmental reasons is rising in some segments — particularly the segments (younger, higher-income, urban) that tend to be early adopters.

**Sustainable sourcing** extends the environmental lens upstream: evaluating suppliers not only on cost and quality but on their environmental and social practices. The brand that makes sustainability claims in its marketing but sources from suppliers with demonstrably poor environmental records produces a contradiction that increasingly surfaces publicly.

The connection to $V = B/P$ is the same as every other chapter. Sustainability commitments raise $B$ for the growing segment of consumers who factor environmental practice into their purchase decisions. They also raise costs. The trade-off is a genuine business decision — not a moral obligation that ignores economics, and not a cost line that ignores the growing segment of customers for whom it matters.

---

## What the Chapter Leaves You With

Distribution is invisible to most customers until it fails. A shipment that arrives late, a product that's out of stock, a return that's frustrating to execute — each of these is a distribution failure, and each erodes $B$ in ways that the product's quality and the brand's advertising can't repair.

The frameworks in this chapter — channel structure, coverage strategy, VMS versus omnichannel, supply chain functions, logistics trade-offs — are all in service of one principle: get the right product to the right customer at the right time. The product P determines what's being delivered. The place P determines whether it gets there.

Outstanding Foods solved their distribution problem before their marketing could work. That order of operations — distribution as a prerequisite for the rest of marketing, not a downstream consequence — is the chapter's central claim.

## Exercises

### Warm-up

**1. Classify the channel.** For each of the following, identify whether the channel is direct or indirect. If indirect, name the intermediary type(s) involved.

- A farmer selling produce directly at a Saturday market.
- A snack brand distributing through a broker to both natural and conventional grocery chains.
- A software company selling enterprise licenses through value-added resellers.
- A watch manufacturer selling through its own branded retail stores only.
- A consumer goods brand selling on Amazon alongside its own DTC website.

*Tests: Objective 2. Difficulty: Low.*

**2. Match the coverage strategy.** For each product and brand, identify whether intensive, selective, or exclusive distribution is most appropriate, and explain the coupling rule that justifies the match in one sentence.

- Coca-Cola Classic.
- A mid-tier athleisure brand targeting gym-going millennials.
- A handcrafted Swiss watchmaker at the $8,000+ price point.
- A new energy drink competing directly with Red Bull.
- Custom-configured enterprise networking hardware.

*Tests: Objective 3. Difficulty: Low.*

**3. Identify the channel system type.** For each, identify VMS, horizontal, multichannel, or omnichannel, and name the specific feature that makes it that type.

- A McDonald's franchise — the franchisor sets standards; the franchisee operates the location.
- A national bank with branches inside Safeway grocery stores.
- A retailer whose customers can order online, try in store, and return through either channel with a single shared account and inventory system.
- A brand selling through its own website, Amazon, and physical retail stores, each managed independently.

*Tests: Objective 4. Difficulty: Low.*

### Application

**4. Diagnose a coverage mismatch.** A premium home goods brand (average product price $150, positioned as "thoughtfully designed everyday objects") sells through its own website, independent specialty retailers, and Amazon Marketplace, where it appears alongside significantly cheaper competitors in the same search results. Apply the coverage-strategy coupling rule: (a) what coverage strategy is this brand using, (b) what coverage strategy does its positioning require, (c) what specific damage is the Amazon channel doing to the brand's $B$, (d) what should the brand do about it — and what would it cost to fix?

*Tests: Objectives 3, 4. Difficulty: Medium.*

**5. Trace the supply chain as a marketing decision.** Dave's Killer Bread's brand promise — organic whole grain nutrition, certified organic ingredients — is a supply chain commitment as much as a marketing claim. Pick a different brand whose promise depends on supply chain integrity: a coffee brand that claims direct-trade sourcing, an apparel brand that claims ethical manufacturing, a food brand with specific ingredient claims. Investigate what the brand claims, whether the supply chain is structured to deliver on those claims, and one specific point where the supply chain could fail to deliver on the promise. Use publicly available information and label inferences.

*Tests: Objective 6. Difficulty: Medium.*

**6. Evaluate the cost-speed trade-off.** A DTC skincare brand currently ships all orders via 2-day air freight to meet customer expectations set by Amazon Prime. Their unit economics are under pressure — fulfillment is now their largest cost category. Evaluate three alternative fulfillment scenarios: (a) shift to 5-day ground shipping, (b) add a second fulfillment warehouse on the opposite coast to reduce transit time while shifting to ground, (c) introduce a tiered shipping option (free 5-day ground, paid 2-day air). For each scenario, describe the cost/speed/customer-satisfaction trade-offs, and recommend which to test first and why.

*Tests: Objective 7. Difficulty: Medium.*

### Synthesis

**7. Design a channel strategy for Outstanding Foods.** The chapter opens with Outstanding Foods' problem: reaching both vegan and mainstream snack buyers requires distribution in two different channel types simultaneously. Using the frameworks from the chapter, design a complete channel strategy: (a) direct or indirect for each audience segment, (b) which coverage strategy for each channel type and why, (c) what channel system type (VMS, horizontal, multichannel, omnichannel) best describes the resulting structure, (d) what supply chain capability is required to serve two distinct retailer types with potentially different order requirements and lead times.

*Tests: Objectives 2, 3, 4, 6. Difficulty: Medium-high.*

**8. Connect distribution to positioning.** Pick a brand that has recently expanded or contracted its distribution — a DTC brand adding retail, a retail brand pulling back from a channel, a luxury brand that went too wide and is now narrowing. Walk the decision through the chapter's frameworks: what coverage strategy did the brand use before, what are they moving toward, what is the positioning signal each sends, and whether the move is consistent with the brand's $V = B/P$ positioning from Chapter 2.

*Tests: Objectives 2, 3, 4. Difficulty: Medium-high.*

### Challenge

**9. The sustainability audit.** Pick a brand with publicly available supply chain information — a brand that makes sustainability claims in its marketing is ideal. Audit the distribution side of those claims: (a) what does the brand claim about its supply chain sustainability (sourcing, logistics, packaging, returns), (b) what evidence is publicly available that the supply chain delivers on those claims, (c) which specific part of the distribution system — last-mile delivery, packaging, returns, upstream sourcing — creates the largest gap between claim and reality, (d) one structural change to the distribution system that would close the largest gap, with an estimated cost implication. Label clearly what's documented versus inferred.

*Tests: Objective 8. Difficulty: High.*
