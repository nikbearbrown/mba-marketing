# Chapter 9 — Marketing in a Global Environment

*Crossing a border is not a translation problem. It is a marketing problem in a country where the firm has no instincts.*

---

In 2000, Starbucks opened its first store in Australia. By 2008, the company had reported losses exceeding $105 million and had closed 61 of its Australian locations.

The failures weren't supply-chain failures or operational failures. The stores opened. The coffee was made. The product was the same product that was working in sixty other countries. The marketing was the same marketing that had worked everywhere else.

That's precisely the problem.

Australia had an established café culture years before Starbucks arrived — espresso-forward, independent, locally owned, built around trained baristas who had made specialty coffee before Starbucks made it fashionable. Starbucks didn't enter a coffee-deprived market. It entered a market that had already solved the problem Starbucks was trying to solve, and asked customers to pay a premium to trade down to a brand they hadn't grown up with. The home-market playbook, applied unchanged, produced an eight-year, hundred-million-dollar lesson about what happens when a firm assumes a new market works like the one it already knows.

The Starbucks Australia story is worth keeping in mind throughout this chapter, because it represents the central failure mode of international marketing: not the failure to translate the campaign, but the failure to recognize that the market is a fundamentally different context — different history, different tastes, different alternatives, different consumer relationships with the product category — and that understanding that context is a precondition for everything else.

---

## Why Cross a Border

The question comes before everything. Firms expand internationally for five overlapping reasons, and the ones that get it right have been clear-eyed about which reason is actually driving the decision.

**Increased revenues.** The most obvious reason and the most common stated rationale. Each new country expands the addressable market. This is real but incomplete as a justification — revenue potential tells you nothing about whether the firm can capture it.

**Decreased competition.** Some markets are saturated; others are not. The US automobile market is fiercely competitive, with most households already owning a vehicle. Developing-country automotive markets have less vehicle penetration and, in some cases, less competition. China is the world's largest vehicle market by both sales and manufacturing output, but it's dominated by domestic automakers — making international entry hard regardless of the potential. Ford is responding to this by launching three new vehicles offered only in China. The market is enormous and almost impenetrable at the same time.

**Faster growth.** When the home market is saturated or growing slowly, international expansion can sustain a growth strategy the domestic market can no longer support. Amazon's Poland fulfillment center employed 25,000 people as of 2022 — a scale of economic effect that didn't exist five years before. Amazon grew into that market because it had more room to grow there than in its established North American base.

**Risk diversification.** Operations in multiple countries allow a firm to absorb a recession in one market with growth in another. The strategic logic is the same as portfolio diversification in finance: don't concentrate all your exposure in one place.

**Surplus disposal.** Before each Super Bowl, the NFL produces winning-team merchandise for *both* teams, so supply is ready the moment the game ends. The losing team's clothing gets donated to other countries — disposing of surplus that would otherwise go to landfill. This is a useful and often overlooked advantage. It's also different from *dumping*, which is selling exports below cost of production to undercut local competitors. Dumping is illegal under WTO rules. Surplus disposal is not.

Any of these five can drive a valid expansion decision. The problem is that firms often pursue expansion because the revenue number looks big, without asking the harder questions: can we compete here, do we understand this market, and is the firm genuinely ready?

The four challenges that answer those questions:

**Understanding the business landscape.** Every market has its own industry structure, competitive intensity, distribution infrastructure, and regulatory environment. The most common trap is ethnocentrism — assuming the new market works like the home market. Starbucks Australia is ethnocentrism in action: the team that approved the expansion assumed that what worked in Seattle would work in Melbourne, because they evaluated the Melbourne opportunity through a Seattle lens.

**Understanding business politics.** Local norms around relationship-building, decision-making, and negotiation vary dramatically by country. A direct-negotiation style that reads as professional in New York reads as rude in markets where extended relationship-building is expected before any business is discussed.

**Differences in regulation.** The United States and New Zealand are the only two countries that allow direct-to-consumer prescription drug advertising. A pharmaceutical firm's US marketing playbook is illegal almost everywhere else on earth.

**Cultural differences.** Nivea's 2017 "White Is Purity" social media campaign was perceived as racially insensitive and then co-opted by white supremacists in their propaganda before Nivea apologized and removed it. Gucci's 2019 balaclava knit jumper — $890, modeled with bright red lips against white skin — drew immediate criticism for resembling blackface and was pulled within hours. Both failures were promotional failures that originated in approval processes where no one asked the question a person from outside the brand's cultural context would have asked immediately. The fix isn't *apologize faster*. It's *don't approve culturally insensitive material in the first place*, which requires getting cross-cultural perspective into the room before launch.

The fundamental tension in all of this is a trade-off between two opposite strategic logics. Same-product-everywhere is cheaper: marketing dollars spread across markets, production benefits from scale, brand consistency builds across borders. Coca-Cola uses essentially the same brand identity worldwide. Different-product-per-market is more relevant: customers receive offerings calibrated to their context, conversion rates rise, cultural failures decrease. Netflix offers different content libraries in each of its markets. Most firms operate somewhere between these poles, and choosing where to sit on that spectrum is one of the first decisions global expansion requires.

## Assessing Whether the Firm Is Ready

The Starbucks Australia failure isn't just a story about misreading the market. It's a story about a firm that expanded before building the instincts needed to succeed in a culturally distinct market. Reading the market correctly is necessary but not sufficient — the firm also has to be capable of acting on what it reads.

Seven internal characteristics distinguish firms that succeed internationally from those that don't. Douglas Quackenbos and coauthors identified them in a *Harvard Business Review* article:

**Attitude** — global expansion is a genuine strategic priority, not a side project. **Aptitude** — the company has the knowledge and skills, not just the ambition. **Magnitude** — the scope of expansion is aligned with capabilities; the firm isn't overcommitting. **Latitude** — the company can adapt; it isn't locked into a rigid home-market template. **Rectitude** — the company has the legal and ethical flexibility to operate differently in different jurisdictions. **Exactitude** — corporate culture can handle the ambiguity that expansion produces. **Fortitude** — there is commitment to the long-term despite setbacks; the firm won't pull out the moment the first quarter disappoints.

These seven form a self-audit. A firm with high aptitude but low fortitude has strong skills and weak staying power — it'll enter a market, hit early friction, and exit before the investment matures. A firm with high magnitude (big ambitions) but low aptitude (weak skills) overcommits and then scrambles. The characteristics interact: you need most of them to be strong for the expansion to succeed.

External conditions are the other half of the readiness question. Four components matter:

**Economic infrastructure.** Roads, railways, ports, telecommunications, internet, electricity. Infrastructure constrains which marketing strategies are even possible. A firm dependent on overnight delivery cannot operate in a market without a delivery network.

**Consumer income — three distinct types.** Consumer income is what a household earns. Disposable income is what's left after taxes. Discretionary income is what's left after taxes *and* essential living expenses — rent, groceries, utilities. This distinction is not subtle. Marketers of luxury goods care about discretionary income; a consumer with high gross income and high essential expenses has little discretionary left. Inflation reshapes this calculus: when essentials cost more, discretionary income shrinks, and luxury marketers feel it first. Procter & Gamble quietly reduced its Charmin Ultra Soft 18-count Mega Package in 2022 from 264 double-ply sheets per roll to 244, with prices holding or rising — shrinkflation, the strategy of keeping prices stable while reducing quantity. It's effective because the change is hard to detect at point of purchase.

<!-- → TABLE: Three types of income (consumer / disposable / discretionary) with definitions, what marketers care about each, and an example product category. The student should see why luxury marketers track discretionary income while CPG marketers track disposable income. -->

**Currency exchange rates.** In 1997, $1.64 bought one British pound. By 2021, $1.37 bought £1.00. The dollar strengthened — US imports to the UK became more expensive for British buyers; UK exports to the US became cheaper for American buyers. Currency moves are structural marketing risks that domestic-only firms never face. A 20 percent currency shift can erase a campaign's profitability without changing anything in the consumer's behavior.

**Political stability and trade regulation.** Stable governments produce predictable regulatory environments and durable foreign investment conditions. In 2022, Russia's invasion of Ukraine triggered widespread corporate withdrawal — McDonald's, which opened its first Russian location in 1990, was among the first global companies to cease operations there, citing both values misalignment and untenable operating conditions. Tariffs (taxes on imports), quotas (limits on import quantities), and embargoes (comprehensive trade prohibitions) can each make a market non-viable regardless of its consumer demand profile.

## How to Enter and What to Change When You Do

A firm that has determined a market is worth entering, and that it is ready to enter, faces a sequence of operational choices that don't exist in domestic marketing.

Six standard entry modes, ordered from least to most involved:

**Exporting** — make the product in one country, sell it in others. Lowest risk; least involvement. The UK's Northampton county — home to Alfred Sargent & Sons, Church's, and John Lobb — saw declining domestic shoemaking businesses revived by exporting traditional English craftsmanship to international markets.

**Licensing** — grant another organization the right to use the firm's name, brand, or trademark on specific items. Lower commitment than franchising; less control as well. Armani licenses its name to companies producing eyeglass frames, fragrances, and watches. The brand collects royalties; the licensee bears manufacturing risk.

**Franchising** — allow another entity to operate using the franchisor's products, branding, and system in exchange for a fee. Over 90 percent of McDonald's globally are franchises owned by independent local businesspeople. Entry cost varies enormously by brand — a McDonald's franchise requires $500,000 in liquid assets, $45,000 in franchise fees, and total expected investment of $1.3M–$2.3M. A Jazzercise franchise costs $1,250 plus liability insurance.

**Joint venture** — two companies create one enterprise or project. Risk-sharing without permanent merger. In 2012, Kellogg's and Wilmar International formed a joint venture giving Kellogg's access to Wilmar's Chinese distribution network in exchange for financial participation.

**Strategic alliance** — two companies invest resources in a mutually beneficial way without forming a single enterprise. Uber and Spotify entered an alliance allowing Uber riders to stream their Spotify accounts during rides. Both firms benefited without merging operations or sharing legal liability.

**Foreign direct investment** — the riskiest and most-involved mode: establish full operations in the target country. Tesla invested in a Shanghai factory and in 2022 announced plans to double original output targets to one million cars annually.

<!-- → DIAGRAM: Six modes of entry on a horizontal axis from least to most involved (Exporting → Licensing → Franchising → Joint Venture → Strategic Alliance → FDI). Below each mode: typical risk level, typical commitment level, and one example brand. -->

Firms typically progress along this spectrum as they gain confidence in a market — start with exporting, move to a joint venture when the market proves viable, eventually invest directly. Skipping steps is possible but raises exposure. The progression is risk management in the form of incremental commitment.

Once entry mode is chosen, the marketing-mix adaptation question becomes operational. The standard label is standardized versus adapted global marketing. Standardized means the same marketing strategy in all markets; Coca-Cola uses the same brand, name, and identity globally. Adapted means strategies differ by market; Netflix adapts content libraries by country. Most firms operate in between, standardizing the brand architecture while adapting individual elements.

For the four Ps in global context: **product** can be extended unchanged (basic electronics often transfer without modification), adapted (McDonald's vegetarian options in India, the regional burger variants from Chapter 7), or invented from scratch for the local market. **Price** is affected by exchange rates, local purchasing power, tariffs, and local production costs — the standard global pricing question is whether to set one global price and absorb currency variation, or set local prices and accept cross-border arbitrage when prices diverge enough to make importing cheaper than buying locally. **Place** depends on distribution infrastructure, which varies enormously. **Promotion** is the most culturally sensitive P — translation, imagery, humor, regulatory environment, and cultural associations all change, and Nivea and Gucci are the cautionary cases.

## Hofstede and the Anatomy of Cultural Friction

The cultural failures described in this chapter — Starbucks Australia, Nivea, Gucci — each originated in assumptions the firm made about the new market without explicitly testing them. Geert Hofstede's cultural dimensions framework, developed in the 1970s through analysis of IBM employees across 50+ countries, gives those assumptions a structure.

Five dimensions:

**Power Distance Index (PDI)** — how much power inequality is accepted by a culture. High PDI cultures (many Asian and Latin American countries) accept hierarchy; marketing should respect status, use authority figures, speak formally to those in power. Low PDI cultures (Nordic countries) share power more evenly; marketing can be more egalitarian. The US scores 40; the world average is 55.

**Individualism vs. Collectivism (IDV)** — whether the culture prioritizes individual goals or group cohesion. The US, UK, and Australia score highly individualist. Many East Asian and Latin American cultures score collectivist. Marketing in collectivist cultures emphasizes group benefits, family, community; in individualist cultures, personal achievement and self-expression.

**Masculinity vs. Femininity (MAS)** — whether the culture values competition, achievement, and material success (masculine) versus cooperation, quality of life, and care for others (feminine). Japan and Italy score high MAS; Sweden and Norway score low. Marketing tone shifts accordingly — achievement-focused campaigns land in high-MAS cultures; they can feel abrasive in low-MAS ones.

**Uncertainty Avoidance (UAI)** — tolerance for ambiguity. High UAI cultures (Greece, Portugal) prefer rules, predictability, and established brands. Low UAI cultures (Singapore, Denmark) tolerate novelty more readily. A new product entering a high UAI market needs more reassurance — certifications, guarantees, track records — than the same product entering a low UAI market.

**Long-term vs. Short-term Orientation (LTO)** — whether the culture prioritizes future rewards versus immediate gratification and tradition. High LTO: East Asian cultures. Low LTO: Western cultures generally. Marketing time horizons and customer-relationship investment patterns shift accordingly.

The discipline is to ask, for each dimension: where does the home market sit, where does the target market sit, and what is the gap? Large gaps signal where cultural friction is likely and where marketing adaptation is necessary. Small gaps signal where existing marketing might transfer with minimal modification.

The Starbucks Italy case, from the chapter's opening context, illustrates the framework in action. Italy is a high-PDI, high-UAI culture with strong traditions around coffee — low IDV relative to the US, high MAS in terms of craft and professional pride. Starbucks entering with its American template would have violated the UAI dimension immediately: a new brand asking Italian consumers to pay premium prices for a foreign version of something they'd been perfecting for decades. Instead, Starbucks opened the Starbucks Reserve Roastery in Milan — a flagship store where you cannot order a Frappuccino but can order wood-fired pizza and cocktails, positioned as a place where Italian customers can *discover* the art and science of coffee. The frame respects Italian coffee culture rather than competing with it. That's Hofstede applied.

The framework is a forecasting tool, not a destiny. It helps the firm anticipate friction; the firm still has to do the localization work. And the work is not optional — Coca-Cola and PepsiCo both struggled when they first entered China, requiring major repositioning to adapt to local taste preferences and distribution structures. 7-Eleven failed in Indonesia by misreading which products and convenience patterns local customers actually valued. In each case, the home-market playbook didn't transfer. The fix was not more marketing spend. It was understanding what specifically didn't transfer and rebuilding for the local context.

## Ethics in International Marketing

International marketing exposes three ethical categories that domestic marketing largely avoids.

**Dumping** — selling exports below cost of production, or below domestic prices, to gain market share and undercut local competitors. Illegal under WTO rules. Distinct from surplus disposal, which is legal. The distinction: surplus disposal moves goods that would otherwise go to waste; dumping destroys local industry for competitive advantage.

**Exploitation** — sourcing labor or materials from markets with weak labor protections or environmental regulations to lower costs. Legally permissible in many jurisdictions; ethically contested. Brands that source this way and don't disclose it face consumer backlash when uncovered — the trust failure mechanism documented in Chapter 2's value ratio ethics section. The consumer's $B$ in $V = B/P$ now includes ethical sourcing for a meaningful and growing share of the market, which means exploitation isn't just an ethical problem. It's a strategic liability.

**"Made in" labeling and country-of-origin** — claims about where products are produced affect perceived quality and ethical positioning. False or misleading origin claims violate consumer-protection laws in most markets and are one of the easier claims to expose in an age of supply-chain transparency.

The discipline isn't to avoid all international risk. It's to understand which risks the firm is exposed to and decide deliberately rather than by default.

International expansion is almost always market development or product diversification.
