# Chapter 1 — Setting the Stage

*Marketing is not a department. It is a way of thinking about how products meet people.*

---

Here is a question that seems simple until you sit with it for a moment.

Why does a cup of coffee cost $1.25 at a gas station and $7.50 at a place with exposed brick and a barista who knows the name of the farm? The liquid inside is, by any objective measure, similar stuff. Beans, hot water, time. The inputs are not twelve times more valuable. Something else is going on.

The easy answer is that people are being fooled — that the exposed brick is a trick and the farm story is theater and the customer is paying for an illusion. But that answer has a problem. If it were right, the gas station would take over the market the moment anyone noticed. And that hasn't happened. Both businesses have been running, side by side, for decades.

So it isn't an illusion. It's something else. Understanding what that something else is — why value isn't a property of the coffee, but of the relationship between the coffee and the person holding it — is, in a single sentence, what this book is about.

---

## What Marketing Actually Is

Marketing has a reputation problem. Ask most people what marketers do and you get a story about advertising — persuasion by repetition, logos, slogans, the art of making people want things they didn't know they wanted. That story isn't false, exactly, but it describes one small corner of a much larger territory.

Here is the fuller description: marketing is the work of understanding what customers actually value and using that understanding to make decisions about products, prices, channels, and messages.

Notice what that definition does and doesn't include. It doesn't say advertising. It doesn't say persuasion. It doesn't say *making people want things*. It says *understanding what they value* — present tense, already existing — and then using that understanding to make decisions.

That reframing matters because it changes who is doing marketing. If marketing is advertising, then marketing is what the marketing department does, and everyone else is off the hook. But if marketing is the work of connecting value to the people who want it, then the engineer who decides how the coffee machine works is doing marketing. The operations team that chose which locations to put the café is doing marketing. The person who designed the cup is doing marketing. The accountant who set the price is doing marketing.

Every decision that ends with a customer is a marketing decision. The department doesn't determine it. The structure of the decision does.

This is the thing I want you to hold onto before anything else in this book: the word "marketing" is not a job title. It's the name of a problem — the problem of how a firm creates value, communicates that value to the people who want it, and captures enough of that value back to keep doing it. The firm that solves that problem well survives. The firm that doesn't, regardless of how good its product is, generally doesn't.

## The Coffee Problem, More Precisely

Let's go back to the coffee, because it's a clean way to see the mechanism.

The gas station coffee solves a problem efficiently. You are tired, you need caffeine, you are already stopping for gas, and the price is low enough that the purchase requires almost no deliberation. The value the gas station is providing is *convenience* and *adequacy* at *low cost*. That's a real offer to a real customer. The gas station has made choices — about location, about price, about product quality — that serve that customer well.

The $7.50 coffee solves a different problem. Maybe it's better coffee, maybe it's not — but even if the quality is roughly equivalent, the customer is paying for other things. The environment. The ritual of ordering from someone who takes coffee seriously. The signal value of being seen in that place with that cup. The experience of a few minutes that feel like a choice, not a transaction. These are real things. They're not illusions. They are the product.

Neither business is wrong. They are serving different customers who value different things. That's the key. *Value is not a property of the product. It's a property of the relationship between the product and the person.*

The formal way to say this: customer value is the ratio of perceived benefits to the price paid.

$$V = \frac{B}{P}$$

where $V$ is perceived value, $B$ is the bundle of benefits the customer believes they're getting, and $P$ is the total price — including money, time, effort, and any friction in the transaction.

Notice what that formula implies. A firm can increase customer value in two ways: increase $B$ or decrease $P$. The gas station chose low $P$. The café chose high $B$. Both produced value for their respective customers. Both are marketing strategies.

Notice also what neither firm can control directly: the numerator $B$ is *perceived* benefits, not actual benefits. A product can be technically excellent and fail to create value if the customer can't perceive the excellence. A product can be technically mediocre and create enormous value if the customer believes otherwise. This is why marketing is not the same as engineering. Engineering optimizes the actual product. Marketing optimizes the relationship between the product and the person's perception of it.

Getting $B/P$ right — for a specific customer, in a specific context — is the central problem of marketing. This book is the toolkit for solving it.

## Four Levers

If the goal is to move the value ratio in the customer's favor, what does the firm actually do? This is where the marketing mix comes in.

There are four variables the firm controls. Product. Price. Place. Promotion.

You will hear these called the "four Ps," and the name is everywhere in marketing education. But I want to be careful about how I introduce them, because the name can make them feel like a checklist — four separate things you do — when they're really four levers that all act on the same ratio.

**Product** is everything the customer receives. The physical object, if there is one. The service around it. The experience of using it. The warranty, the packaging, the design, the brand identity. Every one of these is a decision that shapes the numerator $B$. Every decision about what the product is, and what it isn't, is a decision about what the firm is promising to deliver to the customer.

**Price** is what the customer gives up. Money, obviously. But also time (how long does it take to buy?), effort (how hard is it to use?), psychological friction (is this purchase embarrassing? risky? complicated?). Price is the denominator $P$ in the value equation. Raising price doesn't necessarily lower value — it depends on whether $B$ goes up proportionally. Lowering price doesn't necessarily increase value — if it signals lower quality, it can lower $B$ enough to offset the lower $P$.

**Place** is how the product reaches the customer. The distribution channel — physical stores, websites, wholesale distributors, direct-to-consumer, app stores. Place decisions affect both $B$ and $P$. A product that's inconvenient to get has a higher effective $P$ even at the same price. A product available in a prestigious location gains perceived quality even if nothing about the product changed.

**Promotion** is how the firm communicates what the product offers. Advertising, yes. But also public relations, sales conversations, influencer partnerships, social content, product packaging as communication, word-of-mouth cultivation. Every element of promotion is an attempt to move the customer's estimate of $B$ — to make sure that the value the product actually delivers is the value the customer believes it will deliver, before and after purchase.

Four levers. One ratio. The firm that figures out the right combination for its specific customer wins the specific market it's in. The firm that picks the combination that looks impressive on a slide deck but doesn't match what its actual customers value loses, regardless of how good the individual decisions look in isolation.

## The Shape of the Book

This book is organized around three questions. Not three departments. Not three stages of a process. Three questions, in the order you have to answer them.

**Question one: What is value, and how does a firm decide where to aim it?** Unit 1 (Chapters 1–3) builds the foundation. This chapter sets up the value ratio. Chapter 2 develops it further and shows how it connects to the marketing mix. Chapter 3 shows how a firm uses strategic planning to decide which markets to serve, which products to build, and how to allocate resources across the portfolio.

**Question two: Who are you marketing to, and how do you find out?** Unit 2 (Chapters 4–10) builds the audience layer. Understanding who wants what requires dividing markets into useful segments, choosing which segments to serve, deciding how you want to be perceived by those segments, and gathering the research to verify that your understanding of the audience is accurate. These chapters cover consumer behavior, business buying, segmentation, targeting, positioning, and marketing research.

**Question three: What do you actually do?** Unit 3 (Chapters 11–22) builds the delivery layer. The four Ps in detail, across every category that matters. Product strategy, new product development, pricing mechanics and psychology, the full span of promotion channels, and the distribution channels through which products move from firm to customer.

The order matters. You cannot use the levers effectively if you don't understand the ratio they're acting on. You cannot aim the ratio at the right customers if you don't understand the audience. The book is ordered the way understanding the discipline is ordered.

## What This Book Is Not

I want to say four things clearly, because the framings around marketing are often misleading.

**This is not a course in advertising.** Advertising appears in Chapters 16–19. That is four chapters out of twenty-two. If you came here to learn advertising specifically, this book will give you a useful foundation for thinking about what advertising is supposed to accomplish and when it works — but you will want additional reading to go deep on execution. The ratio $B/P$ determines whether advertising works; advertising itself is one mechanism for moving $B$, and not always the most important one.

**This is not about platform tactics.** The current Meta ad-format playbook will be different by the time you read this. The current Google Ads bidding strategy will be different. The book teaches the underlying mechanisms — how value works, how price signals quality, how channels move products, why customers buy what they buy — that hold across platforms and across decades. Once you understand the mechanisms, you can learn current platform tactics in an afternoon. The reverse is not true: a practitioner who knows the current TikTok algorithm but doesn't understand why value is a ratio and not a fixed property of the product is permanently dependent on someone else to think for them.

**This is not a course in selling.** Personal selling is one chapter (Chapter 18). The other twenty-one chapters are not about getting individual customers to say yes to individual transactions. They are about the upstream decisions — what to build, for whom, at what price, distributed how, communicated through which channels — that determine whether the customer encounter is likely to end in yes in the first place.

**This is not skeptical of marketing as a discipline.** Marketing has a reputation for manipulation, and some of that reputation is earned — by specific practitioners who used the tools badly or dishonestly. I treat marketing as serious work worth doing well. I also treat unethical marketing practices as failure modes worth naming, not because they're disreputable but because they're usually also strategically self-defeating. A firm that misleads customers about $B$ incurs a future cost — returns, complaints, damaged reputation, erosion of repurchase — that can exceed the short-term gain from the deception. The ethical case and the strategic case, here, usually point in the same direction.

## How to Read This Chapter As The Start of Something

Every chapter in this book follows the same structure. Learning objectives at the front — specific, testable things you'll be able to do. A section that names the payoff in plain terms. Three concept sections, each with a motivating puzzle, the mechanism, the tradeoff the mechanism creates, a worked example, and the places where students typically go wrong. An integration section. Graduated exercises. A summary that names the one idea most worth keeping. Connections to what comes next.

This chapter is shorter than those that follow, deliberately. It does one thing: it clears the assumption that marketing is what the marketing department does. With that assumption cleared, every subsequent chapter lands differently. The segmentation chapter in Unit 2 is not a technique for marketing people — it's a method for any decision-maker trying to understand which customers value what. The pricing chapter in Unit 3 is not about setting prices — it's about understanding what price communicates to a customer about $B$, and how to use that signal intentionally.

Everything else in this book is a refinement of the value ratio and the four levers. This chapter is why that ratio exists and what it's for.

## Connections Forward

**Chapter 2 — Marketing and Customer Value** takes the ratio $V = B/P$ seriously as a working tool. It unpacks what "perceived benefits" actually means — why the numerator is a bundle of functional, social, and emotional benefits rather than a single number — and shows how each of the four Ps acts on that bundle. By the end of Chapter 2 you will be able to map any product's marketing strategy onto the value equation and identify which levers the firm is pulling and which it's ignoring.

**Chapter 3 — Strategic Planning in Marketing** turns the value model into a decision process. A firm doesn't aim at all customers simultaneously — it chooses. Chapter 3 is about how firms make that choice deliberately: which markets to enter, which products to invest in, how to allocate limited resources across a portfolio of opportunities, and how to set goals that connect daily marketing decisions to the firm's long-term direction.

## Exercises

### Warm-Up

**1.** The value ratio is $V = B/P$. A streaming service raises its monthly price from $9.99 to $15.99 but adds a library of 500 new films. Has customer value gone up, down, or is it impossible to say? Explain what additional information you would need to answer with confidence. *(Tests: understanding that $B$ is perceived, not actual; that the ratio moves on both sides simultaneously.)*

**2.** Name three decisions a hospital makes that are marketing decisions under the definition in this chapter — even though no one in the hospital calls them that. For each, identify which of the four Ps it belongs to. *(Tests: that marketing decisions are defined by their structure, not by who makes them.)*

**3.** A firm lowers its price by 20%. Under what conditions does this increase customer value? Under what conditions does it decrease it? *(Tests: the price-quality signal embedded in $P$, and the possibility that lowering $P$ can lower $B$.)*

### Application

**4.** Pick one product you purchased in the last month — anything from a coffee to a software subscription to a piece of clothing. Map it onto the value equation: what was in your $B$ bundle (list at least three distinct benefits, functional and non-functional), and what was in your $P$ (list every cost beyond money). Then identify which of the four Ps the firm used most aggressively to win your purchase. *(Tests: ability to decompose $B$ into a bundle; ability to see $P$ as more than price.)*

**5.** Two gyms operate in the same neighborhood. One charges $25/month with basic equipment and no classes. The other charges $120/month with premium equipment, group classes, and a juice bar. Both are profitable and have been operating for ten years. Using the value ratio and the four Ps, explain why neither has driven the other out of business. *(Tests: that different $B/P$ configurations can coexist when they serve different customers.)*

**6.** The chapter argues that "engineering optimizes the actual product; marketing optimizes the relationship between the product and the person's perception of it." Give one example where these two goals align perfectly and one example where they create tension inside a firm. *(Tests: understanding the distinction between actual and perceived benefits, and the organizational friction that can create.)*

### Synthesis

**7.** A startup founder tells you: "We have the best product in our category. We just need to get the word out." Using the framework from this chapter, identify everything that could be wrong with that sentence — not as a critique of the founder, but as a map of what they haven't yet considered. *(Tests: integration of $B/P$ ratio with the four Ps; the distinction between quality and perceived quality; the question of which customer's $B$ is being optimized.)*

**8.** The chapter introduces three units: Foundation, Audience, Levers. A firm launches a product, finds it's not selling, and immediately invests in advertising (Unit 3 lever). Based on the architecture described in this chapter, what Unit 1 and Unit 2 questions should the firm have answered first — and what would each answer have changed about the advertising decision? *(Tests: understanding why the book is sequenced the way it is; ability to reason about upstream decisions and downstream consequences.)*

### Challenge

**9.** Choose a company that has failed in the last ten years — one you know well enough to have an opinion about. Reconstruct its failure using only the vocabulary from this chapter: the value ratio, the four Ps, and the three-unit sequence (Foundation, Audience, Levers). Which part of the framework did they get wrong first? What happened downstream as a result? Limit yourself to 400 words. *(Tests: ability to apply the framework analytically to a real case; distinguishes students who understood the machinery from students who memorized the terms.)*

**The Prompt:**

```
I'm building an AI Marketing Assistant as a semester-long project.
The assistant will be a Claude Project I keep adding capabilities to
as my marketing course progresses. By Chapter 22 it should be a real
working tool — not a toy.

I need help setting it up. Walk me through this by asking ONE

1. The brand/product the assistant will serve. One specific
   sentence — "an indie sneaker brand making one model, sold direct

4. The three things the assistant should be GOOD at by Chapter 22

After all five answers, write the initial **Project Instructions**

- Who the assistant serves and their typical questions.

  "you need a human expert here, not me."

End with three TEST PROMPTS I can run against the assistant to
verify it's behaving correctly:
- One that should produce a useful answer.
- One that should trigger a refusal.
- One that should trigger an escalation to a human expert.

Output the Project Instructions as a clean markdown block I can
paste directly into Claude's project-level instructions field, plus
the three test prompts below it.
```

---

**What this produces:** A ready-to-paste Claude Project system prompt + three test prompts to verify the assistant is calibrated correctly. Save the system prompt to your Claude Project's project-level instructions field. Run the three test prompts and document the results in your running notes.

**How to adapt this prompt:**

- *For your own project:* Pick a brand you actually know something about. The assistant gets sharper the more domain knowledge you can supply during setup. A real brand you've watched closely beats a fictional one; a fictional brand in a domain you understand beats either of those if the real-brand option is one you don't actually care about.
- *For ChatGPT / Gemini:* Works as written. Build a Custom GPT instead of a Claude Project; the system prompt goes in the GPT's instructions field.
- *For Claude Code:* Wrong tool — this is a setup conversation, not a code task.
- *For a Claude Project:* The whole project IS a Claude Project. The system prompt you produce here is the project's project-level instructions.

**Connection to previous chapters:** This is the project's opening. Every later chapter adds a capability to this assistant.
