# Chapter 2 — Marketing and Customer Value

*The ratio in your head, the levers in the firm's hands, and the room they both operate in.*

Here is something that puzzled me for a long time.

In the United States, one sports drink holds 67.7 percent of its market. Not 30 percent, not 40 — sixty-seven point seven. The next-largest competitor has 13.7 percent. The one after that has 9.3 percent. The category has been this lopsided for fifty-five years. The dominant brand was first formulated by researchers at the University of Florida in 1965.

The drink is Gatorade. The runner-up is Powerade, made by Coca-Cola — one of the most powerful marketing companies on earth.

Now here is the thing that makes this genuinely puzzling rather than just interesting: Gatorade is not dramatically cheaper than Powerade. In a blind taste test, most people cannot reliably tell the two apart. The ingredients are not secret, proprietary, or difficult to manufacture. And yet the ratio is five to one, and it has been five to one, more or less, for longer than most of the people reading this have been alive.

Something is producing that ratio. The question is what. And once you understand what, you understand most of what marketing actually is.

## Value Is a Number the Customer Computes

Let me give you the model first, and then we will find out how deep it goes.

Customer value is a fraction:

$$V = \frac{B}{P}$$

$B$ is the benefits the customer perceives. $P$ is the price they pay. The customer, without knowing they are doing arithmetic, computes this ratio every time they consider a purchase. The product with the higher ratio wins, provided the customer can see the difference.

I want to dwell on the word *perceives*, because it is carrying most of the weight.

$B$ is not a spec sheet. It is not the number of electrolytes per ounce or the calorie count on the back panel. It is the customer's *judgment about what the product is worth to them* — before they look at the price. Two products can be functionally identical and have wildly different $B$ values in the same customer's head. The spec sheet would not predict this. The model does.

Now let's open the numerator and see what it's made of. The $B$ the customer perceives has four layers:

**Functional value** is what the product does. Gatorade hydrates you and replaces electrolytes. So does Powerade. Both score similarly here. If functional value were the whole story, the market would be close to even, and the category would compete mostly on price. It doesn't.

**Monetary value** is what the price *feels like* — not the number itself, but the customer's sense of whether they got a deal or got taken. This is subtler than it sounds. A $4 coffee can feel like a bargain to someone who cares about origin and craft and was expecting to pay $6. The same $4 can feel expensive to someone who wanted caffeine and nothing else. Monetary value is a perception, not a calculation.

**Social value** is what the product signals to other people. When you put Gatorade on your gym bag, you are telling the people around you something. The GOAT Camp commercial — the one where Michael Jordan and Serena Williams are waiting on a misted training field to mentor the next generation of athletes — is almost entirely an argument about social value. It says: *this is what elite athletes drink*. The customer who carries Gatorade is borrowing some of that association.

**Psychological value** is what the product says to *you about yourself*. The GOAT Camp customer is not just signaling to the room. They are telling themselves a story: *I am someone who trains the right way*. The drink is a prop in that story. The customer will never type "makes me feel like Michael Jordan" into a product search. But that story is doing real work in the purchase decision.

<!-- → INFOGRAPHIC: Stack the four value layers (functional → monetary → social → psychological) as a pyramid. Label each layer with the Gatorade example. Student should see that the functional layer is the smallest driver of the purchase, not the largest. -->

All four layers live inside the $B$. Gatorade's fifty-five-year lead is not an ingredient lead. It is a $B$ lead — built layer by layer across fifty-five years of marketing investment that no competitor can simply purchase and install.

Here is the useful form of the model. To move $V$ in your favor — to make a customer more likely to choose your product — you have two and only two options:

1. Raise $B$. Add a real benefit, build a brand story, earn a social association, create a psychological narrative.
2. Cut $P$. Lower the price.

These look symmetric in the fraction but are profoundly asymmetric in practice. Cutting price is fast, visible, and reversible — but you lose margin on every sale, and competitors can copy it tomorrow morning. If you cut and they cut, you both lose margin and nothing has changed in the customer's head. Raising $B$ is slow and expensive, but the brand associations that Gatorade has accumulated over five decades are not available for purchase at any price. Powerade could spend $10 billion and not buy the GOAT Camp moment, because that moment only lands the way it does because of what Gatorade has *meant for five decades*. History is not for sale.

This is why dominant market positions persist. The firms that hold them have been doing $B$ work, not $P$ work. They raised the numerator instead of racing to zero on the denominator.

## What Customers Actually Want (And Why They Can't Quite Tell You)

The model gets more interesting when you try to figure out what $B$ you are building.

Customers are surprisingly unreliable reporters of what they want. Not because they are dishonest — because *wanting* is layered, and most customers only have access to the top layer. Marketers have found it useful to distinguish five levels:

**Stated needs** are what the customer says. *I want a sports drink.* This is the request as typed into a search bar. It is the surface.

**Real needs** are the specific version of the stated need that actually matters. *I want something that hydrates faster than water and doesn't taste like a pill.* The customer may not say this, but it is what they mean. Answering the stated need without the real need produces products that technically satisfy the request and still disappoint.

**Unstated needs** are what the customer expects but would never think to specify. *I expect the cap to reseal without leaking.* No one praises this in a review when it works. Everyone mentions it when it doesn't. Violate an unstated need and the customer feels betrayed, even though they never told you about it.

**Delight needs** are what would genuinely surprise the customer in a good way. A flavor they didn't know existed. A feature they didn't know to ask for. Customers don't express these needs because they haven't imagined the solution yet. When you find them, customers talk about them — which is the free promotion that no ad budget can buy.

**Secret needs** are what the customer will not admit, even to themselves. *I want to feel like a serious athlete*. The GOAT Camp argument, again. The customer will never say this is what they're paying for. It is the most important thing they are paying for.

The famous instruction to "just ask customers what they want" reaches only the stated layer. Everything below that requires inference, observation, and honest thinking about human motivation. The firms that build durable $B$ advantages are almost always working on the bottom two levels — delight and secret — while competitors are still racing to answer the stated need faster and cheaper.

## Four Levers That Must Pull Together

So you understand value. You know what the customer is computing and why. Now you need to move the number. A firm has four tools.

**Product** is the thing being offered — the good, the service, the experience. Product decisions include what to build, how to design the packaging, what variants to offer. Product is the part most people associate with the word "company."

**Price** is what the customer pays — and not just the number, but the structure. A product priced at $100 with twelve-month zero-interest financing is a different offer than the same product at $100 cash. A subscription is a different offer than a one-time purchase. Price is a signal as much as it is a cost. A $500 watch sold at $100 is not perceived as a bargain — it is perceived as suspicious.

**Place** is where the customer can get the product. This is not the firm's address; it is the customer's access. Distribution channels, retail partners, the firm's own website, third-party marketplaces — all the paths from the product to the customer's hands. A Toyota configured and ordered online at 11 p.m. from a couch is a different place strategy than the same Toyota sold at a dealership.

**Promotion** is everything the firm does to tell customers the product exists and persuade them to want it. Advertising. Public relations. Social media. In-store displays. The GOAT Camp commercial.

<!-- → DIAGRAM: Four-quadrant diagram with the 4Ps labeled. Add two arrows from each P to each adjacent P — showing that each lever affects the others. Not a simple grid; a connected system. -->

Here is the principle that matters most about these four levers: they are not independent. They are a system. Each one exerts pressure on the others, and they must all point in the same direction for the customer to read them as a coherent offer.

Let me show you this with an example that worked.

In May 2022, the fourth season of *Stranger Things* arrived on Netflix. JanSport released five backpacks themed to the show — the Hawkins crest, the Hellfire Club, the Upside Down. Premium-priced relative to the standard JanSport line. Sold through JanSport's own channels and carefully selected partners. Promoted almost entirely by the cultural wave the show created — JanSport pointed at the moment; it did not manufacture the moment. The backpacks sold out.

Run the four levers and see why.

The *product* rebuilt $B$ from scratch. The functional benefit — carrying your stuff — is identical to any other JanSport. The social and psychological value has been completely replaced. The bag is now a fandom signal. The customer is buying membership in the world of the show.

The *price* is premium. This is the only correct move. The fan merchandise market prices on fandom intensity, not production cost. A fan item priced like a commodity is not a tribute to the thing you love — it is a cash grab. The premium price says: *we understand what this means to you*.

The *place* is selective. Not flooded into discount stores. The scarcity is part of the product. A fan item that is everywhere is no longer special.

The *promotion* borrows Netflix's engine. JanSport does not have the budget to manufacture a cultural moment. It does not need to — the cultural moment already exists. The correct promotional strategy when you cannot outspend Netflix is to be present and legible in the current Netflix generates.

<!-- → TABLE: 4P × Evaluation table. Columns: P | Decision made | Why it fits the posture | What would break it. The "what would break it" column is the pedagogy — it forces the student to see each P as a choice with a failure mode, not just a description. -->

Now test the consistency. If JanSport had priced the bags at $40, the premium $B$ would have collapsed. If they had put the bags in every supermarket, the scarcity signal would have evaporated. If the bags had been generic JanSport designs in Hawkins colors, fans would have noticed — and said so publicly — on the same social platforms that made the collaboration visible.

All four had to lock together. They did. That is why it worked. The discipline of the 4Ps is not filling in four boxes. It is asking whether the four boxes are *telling one coherent story* to one coherent customer. If they are not, the customer doesn't experience a marketing problem — they experience confusion about what the brand is. Confusion is death.

## The Room the Levers Operate In

Here is the question the 4Ps leave open: what makes certain moves available and others impossible?

The answer is environment. Every marketing decision a firm makes happens inside three nested layers of context, only one of which the firm controls.

The innermost layer is the **internal environment** — what the firm actually has. Marketers group these into five categories: Minds (the people, their skills and judgment), Minutes (time, schedules, how fast the organization can move), Machinery (equipment, technology, what can actually be built), Materials (the inputs the firm buys to make what it sells), and Money (the financial resources available). These are the firm's assets and its constraints simultaneously. A firm with strong Minds and no Money can plan strategies it cannot execute. A firm with Money and weak Minds can execute strategies that turn out to be wrong.

The internal environment also includes organizational culture — the accumulated habits, values, and unspoken rules that determine "how we do things here." Culture shows up in the marketing message whether the firm manages it or not. When *The Ellen DeGeneres Show* faced public allegations in 2020 of a toxic workplace — harassment, intimidation, racist treatment — the brand collapsed. Not because DeGeneres changed, but because the internal reality that the brand was hiding became visible. Internal culture is a marketing variable. Most firms don't manage it as one until it becomes a crisis.

The middle layer is the **microenvironment** — the ring of actors the firm interacts with directly but does not control. Suppliers who provide inputs; market intermediaries like Walmart and Target who stand between the brand and the customer; the customers themselves; competitors; and the general public whose perception of the firm is an asset the firm doesn't own.

The outer layer is the **macroenvironment** — forces no single firm can change, which sets the ceiling on what is possible. The standard scan covers six dimensions: Political (trade policy, regulatory load, tax structure), Economic (inflation, employment, consumer purchasing power), Social (demographics, cultural norms, shifting values), Technological (new capabilities), Legal (what the firm is allowed to say and do), and Environmental (climate expectations, resource constraints, emissions regulations).

The important thing about these three layers is how their controllability relates to their importance. The internal environment is mostly under the firm's direct control. The microenvironment can be influenced through relationships and contracts but not moved by decree. The macroenvironment is essentially fixed for any single firm at any single moment.

You might think this means the outer layers matter less. The opposite is true. The macroenvironment matters most *because* it is hardest to change. It determines which moves are even possible before the firm makes a single decision.

In 2021, Tesla moved its corporate headquarters from California to Texas. So did Hewlett Packard Enterprise. So did Oracle. The stated reasons — lower taxes, lower housing costs, lighter regulations — are all macroenvironment factors. None of them is within any single firm's control. All of them changed what was possible. Lower operating costs mean the existing product can generate more margin, or the next product can be priced lower to open a new segment, or the firm can invest more in $B$-raising. The macroenvironment move expanded the marketing option set.

Here is the discipline this implies: spend most of your *decision-making energy* on what you control, and most of your *attention* on what you cannot. A firm with a superbly optimized internal environment, running headfirst into a macroeconomic recession, will lose. A firm with mediocre internals riding a strong demographic tailwind will win for a decade before it even knows what it's doing right.

## How This Loops Together

Here is the whole picture assembled in one place.

The customer carries a ratio: $V = B/P$. The $B$ is layered across four value types and shaped by five levels of need, most of which the customer cannot cleanly articulate. The firm pulls four levers — product, price, place, promotion — to move that ratio in its favor. The levers must point together or the customer reads them as noise. And all of this happens inside three nested environments that constrain which moves are available to begin with.

This did not arrive fully formed. It was built over roughly 175 years of firms getting it wrong and slowly figuring out what they had missed.

In 1850, a watch company in Waltham, Massachusetts became the first in the United States to mass-produce watches using division of labor. The implicit marketing posture of the era: *if we build it cheaply enough and distribute it widely enough, people will buy it*. Availability was the problem. Availability was the solution.

By the early twentieth century, availability was largely solved. Firms started competing on quality and features — the better product won. The failure mode was quiet but fatal. Firms fell so in love with their products that they stopped paying attention to what customers actually needed. Theodore Levitt named this in his 1960 essay *Marketing Myopia*: American railroads lost passengers not because transportation demand declined, but because they thought they were in the railroad business rather than the transportation business. They optimized the product and ignored the customer.

Then supply outran demand. Firms pivoted to aggressive selling — push hard, advertise loud, close the deal. This worked for as long as customers had no alternatives and no good way to share negative experiences with one another.

The marketing concept inverted the logic of everything before it. Instead of making something and finding someone to buy it: *start with the customer's needs, build the product*. The 4Ps and $V = B/P$ both assume this orientation.

The most recent layer adds a third variable. The firm must balance the customer's wants, its own capabilities, and society's long-term interests. TOMS Shoes, founded in 2006, built its company on this model: for every pair sold, the company donates a pair to a child in need. TOMS reports more than 95 million pairs delivered to children in 82 countries. The donation is not separate from the marketing. The donation *is* the marketing — it is how TOMS builds a $B$ the customer can feel good about carrying.

## The Boundary

The 4Ps and $V = B/P$ give the firm enormous leverage to shape $B$ in the customer's head. The persistent temptation is to inflate $B$ dishonestly — to claim benefits that don't exist, to imply endorsements that were never given, to manufacture urgency that isn't real.

Living Essentials, LLC — the maker of 5-Hour Energy — advertised its product as "doctor-recommended" and superior to traditional caffeine. The claims were found misleading. The company was ordered to pay $4.3 million in damages.

This is not merely a legal risk. It is a mathematical one. Mintel research found that 56 percent of U.S. consumers stop buying from companies they believe are unethical. Twenty-nine percent share their negative experience via social media, which means the loss propagates. One trust failure, now visible to everyone, kills more $B$ than ten good campaigns can rebuild.

The federal Lanham Act allows competitors to sue for false advertising — so false comparative claims carry legal exposure as well as reputational exposure.

Ethics is not a campaign you run in Q4. It is the boundary inside which every other marketing decision happens. $V = B/P$ gives you the math. Ethics defines the domain over which the math is allowed to operate. Cross the boundary and the model breaks — not in the equation, but in the customer's trust, which is the only place it actually runs.

## What You Can Now Do

Before this chapter, the Gatorade puzzle — 67.7 percent market share, fifty-five years, a competitor backed by Coca-Cola — probably looked like a branding mystery or a historical accident. It is neither. It is $B$ work, accumulated and compounded.

You can now run three models on any market simultaneously. First: what is the value ratio the customer is computing, and which of the four value layers is doing the most work? Second: are the four levers telling one coherent story, or two inconsistent ones? Third: which environment layer is most constraining what the firm can do right now, and is the firm paying attention to it?

The one thing to carry forward: $V = B/P$ is a ratio the customer computes, not a number the firm sets. The firm can influence perception. It cannot dictate it. Every marketing decision is a bet about what will move perception in the customer's head — and the customer's head is doing arithmetic the firm cannot fully observe.

This is humbling. It is also the reason marketing exists as a discipline. If perception were transparent and controllable, you would not need marketing. You would just need a product.

## Exercises

### Warm-up

**1. Decode a purchase you made this week.** Pick something you bought recently — coffee, a piece of clothing, an app subscription, anything. Write one sentence for each of the four value layers (functional, monetary, social, psychological) describing what that layer contributed to your decision. Then identify which single layer did the most work. Was it the obvious one? If not, what does that tell you about how the product is marketed?

*Tests: the four-layer model of $B$. Difficulty: Low.*

**2. Identify the need level.** For each of the following customer statements, name the highest need level being expressed — stated, real, unstated, delight, or secret — and explain your reasoning in one sentence.

- "I just want a gym bag that holds my stuff."
- "I bought the Yeti because it keeps my coffee hot for six hours."
- "I got the same running shoes the elites use."
- "I didn't expect the app to remember my preferences between sessions — that was a nice surprise."

*Tests: the five-level need framework. Difficulty: Low.*

**3. Name the marketing orientation.** In two to three sentences each, identify which marketing concept — production, product, sales, marketing, or societal — best describes each of the following firms, and say what evidence in the description points there.

- A craft brewery that releases whatever the head brewer finds interesting, keeps no tap handles in chain restaurants, and expects customers to seek them out.
- A software company that cold-emails 10,000 small businesses per week with the same pitch regardless of whether they have expressed interest.
- A consumer electronics brand that starts every product cycle by interviewing customers about unmet frustrations, then builds to address the top-ranked one.

*Tests: the evolution of marketing concepts. Difficulty: Low.*

### Application

**4. Run $V = B/P$ on a real competition.** Choose two products competing in the same category — two streaming services, two protein bars, two project management tools. For each product, estimate $B$ on a 0–100 scale by scoring all four value layers (assign weights if you like). Look up or estimate $P$. Compute $V = B/P$ for each.

Does the higher-$V$ product have the higher market share? If yes, the model predicted the outcome. If no, what is the model missing — a value layer, a distribution advantage, a switching-cost barrier? Name it specifically.

*Tests: $V = B/P$ as a predictive tool; forces confrontation with the model's limits. Difficulty: Medium.*

**5. Find the broken mix.** Identify a product whose four Ps are telling inconsistent stories — a premium-priced item sold in a discount channel, an aspirational brand running price-led advertising, a mass-market product in boutique-only distribution. Document the specific inconsistency: which P contradicts which other P, and how would a customer experience the contradiction? Then propose a fix — not "align the Ps" but a specific change to one or two levers that would restore coherence.

*Tests: the 4Ps as a system, not a checklist. Difficulty: Medium.*

**6. Scan one factor that dominates.** Pick a brand operating in a category where the macroenvironment is visibly shifting — fast fashion, ride-sharing, print media, electric vehicles. Run a PESTLE scan, write one sentence per factor, then cross out the four factors that are currently background noise and circle the one or two that are actually constraining what the brand can do right now. For each circled factor: is the brand's current marketing running with the change or against it?

*Tests: the three-environment framework; breaks the fill-every-box PESTLE habit. Difficulty: Medium.*

### Synthesis

**7. Connect the environments to the mix.** Apply the three-environment framework to the JanSport × *Stranger Things* collaboration. For the internal environment: which of the 5Ms was the most critical resource JanSport needed to execute the collaboration, and which was likely the most constrained? For the microenvironment: which single actor outside JanSport's control most determined whether the collaboration succeeded or failed? For the macroenvironment: which PESTLE factor best explains why branded entertainment collaborations became a viable marketing strategy at all?

Then draw the chain: trace how one environment factor you identified directly shaped one specific P in JanSport's mix. Show the link explicitly.

*Tests: integration of the environment model with the 4Ps. Difficulty: Medium-high.*

**8. Apply the ethics boundary.** A fitness supplement brand wants to run advertising claiming its product is "the most trusted pre-workout in America" and "clinically proven to improve performance." Evaluate each claim: is it puffery, a verifiable factual claim, or a potentially false claim? What would the brand need to be able to demonstrate to defend each claim if a competitor brought a Lanham Act challenge?

Now write two alternative taglines that make the strongest honest case for the product's $B$ without crossing the legal or ethical line. For each, identify which need level — stated, real, unstated, delight, or secret — the tagline is targeting.

*Tests: ethics as a $B$ constraint, not a separate topic; Lanham Act; need-level identification. Difficulty: Medium-high.*

### Challenge

**9. Build the full picture for a product that fails.** Find a product that has genuinely struggled — declining sales, failed launch, loss of market share. Reconstruct what went wrong using all three frameworks from this chapter: $V = B/P$ (which layer of $B$ eroded, or did $P$ move without a corresponding $B$ move?), the 4Ps (was there a consistency failure in the mix?), and the three environments (did a macroenvironment shift make the product's previous $B$ irrelevant?). Use verifiable public information — sales data, press coverage, the company's own statements.

The goal is not a list of problems. It is one coherent explanation: here is the root cause, here is how the three frameworks each see it, and here is what a different marketing decision at a specific moment would have changed.

*Tests: synthesis of all three frameworks in a real-world diagnostic. Difficulty: High.*
