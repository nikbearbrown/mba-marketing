# Chapter 13 — Maintaining a Competitive Edge with New Offerings

*New products are how firms grow. The discipline of getting from idea to commercial launch — without losing too much money or stealing someone else's intellectual property — is most of the work.*

---

In April 2011, Mark Levin and Michael Dubin — two men who had met at a party — launched a website and posted a video. The video was funny. It explained that razor blades were too expensive and that their company, Dollar Shave Club, would mail you blades for a dollar a month.

The video went viral. The website promptly crashed from traffic.

Five years later, Unilever acquired Dollar Shave Club for $1 billion.

Gillette, the incumbent, had spent decades building one of the strongest brand positions in consumer goods. Its razors were the default male grooming choice in most of the world. The product was good. The distribution was excellent. The marketing was substantial. None of it prevented a two-person startup with a funny video from taking a measurable slice of a market Gillette had dominated for a generation.

The Dollar Shave Club case is studied so often in marketing courses that it risks becoming cliché, so let me be precise about what made it work. It wasn't the video. The video attracted attention, but attention doesn't build a billion-dollar company. What worked was that Levin and Dubin had identified three things customers actually believed: Gillette's prices were too high, in-store shaving-supply shopping was annoying, and subscription models were becoming a normal way to buy recurring-use products. The video communicated all three insights in ninety seconds. The product delivered on all three. The marketing and the offering were aligned.

That alignment — between what the market actually believes, what the product actually delivers, and what the marketing actually communicates — is the central discipline of new product development. And it is, far more often than luck or creativity, the thing that explains why some new products succeed and most don't.

---

## What "New Product" Actually Means

The first thing to establish is that "new product" is not a single category. A firm launching a product that has never existed in any form is doing something fundamentally different from a firm launching a new flavor of an existing snack. Both are new products in a loose sense. Their development processes, marketing strategies, and risk profiles are almost nothing alike.

Five distinct categories:

**New-to-the-world products** — genuinely novel categories that didn't exist before. The first iPhone in 2007. Tesla's first Roadster. These require the heaviest customer education; the customer has no prior experience with the category and no existing comparison set. The marketing job is partly informational — explaining what the product is before you can explain why to buy it.

**New product lines** — entry into a category that exists in the world but is new to the firm. Apple entering the watch market. The category is established; the firm is the new entrant. The firm needs to explain why its version of an existing thing is worth choosing over what's already there.

**Additions to existing product lines** — new variants within a familiar line. New Oreo flavors, new iPhone storage tiers, seasonal editions. The brand is established; the firm is extending its range. Marketing leverages existing brand equity while signaling what's new about this particular variant.

**Improvements and revisions** — better versions of products already sold. Each successive iPhone generation. Tide's more than fifty formulation changes in forty years. The customer relationship is established; the marketing challenge is communicating what improved without undermining the credibility of the previous version.

**Repositionings** — the same product aimed at a new use or segment. Arm & Hammer baking soda repositioned as a refrigerator deodorizer. The product itself didn't change; the customer's mental model of what it's for did. This is a marketing innovation rather than a product innovation, and it can be enormously profitable at relatively low development cost.

The classification matters before you can decide anything else about the new product, because each category has a different answer to the question of how much customer education the marketing needs to do, how much the firm can leverage existing relationships, and what kind of competition it will face.

## The Seven Stages from Idea to Shelf

Most new products fail. The commonly cited failure rate for new consumer products is somewhere between fifty and eighty percent depending on how you define failure and which industry you're measuring. That number is high enough that the firms succeeding at new product development consistently are clearly doing something different — not getting luckier, but running a more disciplined process.

The standard framework is a seven-stage development process. (Some references extend it to eight by splitting commercialization; the substance is identical.) The stages are sequential, each designed to test a specific set of assumptions before the next, more expensive stage begins.

**Stage 1 — Idea Generation.** Generate as many raw ideas as possible. Internal sources — R&D, market research teams, employees across every function — and external sources — customers, suppliers, distributors, competitive observation. At this stage, quantity is the priority. Ideas that seem unlikely are still worth generating; the screening stage comes next.

**Stage 2 — Idea Screening and Evaluation.** Filter. The questions here are simple: Does this idea fit the firm's strategy? Does it address a real customer need? Could it be produced and priced profitably? Weak ideas eliminated here cost almost nothing. The same ideas, if they survive to commercialization before being killed, cost enormously.

**Stage 3 — Concept Development and Testing.** An idea becomes a concept when it's specific enough to react to. The yogurt powder idea from the source material could become three different concepts: an instant on-the-go breakfast drink, a high-protein snack to mix with juice or milk, or a post-workout protein replacement for athletes. Each concept has different competitors, different target customers, and different price points. Concept testing presents these alternatives to actual target customers and observes which one they respond to — ideally *before* any production investment is made.

This is the most underinvested stage in most failed product launches. The cost of concept testing is a small fraction of the cost of full product development. The information gained is often the single most valuable input to everything that follows. Firms that skip it, or compress it to a few informal conversations, are substituting overconfidence for learning.

**Stage 4 — Marketing Strategy Development.** For the winning concept, develop a complete marketing plan: target market, positioning, planned price, distribution strategy, promotion approach, projected sales. This stage runs in parallel with or just ahead of Stage 5 — because the product decisions and marketing decisions are not independent.

**Stage 5 — Business Analysis.** Project revenues, costs, and profits across a planning horizon. Determine whether the financial case justifies the investment. A technically interesting concept that can't be produced at a price the target segment will pay fails here. Better to discover this before product development than after.

**Stage 6 — Product Development.** Create a physical prototype or functional service version. Test for performance against the concept's specifications, for manufacturability, for unit cost. This is the stage that most people think of when they imagine product development — but note that it comes after the concept has been tested, the marketing strategy sketched, and the financial case validated. The expensive engineering work begins only after the cheaper conceptual work has confirmed there's something worth building.

**Stage 7 — Market Testing.** A limited rollout to a sample market. Real consumers, real prices, real shelf or digital placement. The goal is to observe actual purchasing behavior rather than intended purchasing behavior. Survey respondents reliably overstate their purchase intent; market test buyers tell you what they actually do with money in hand. The gap between stated intent and actual behavior is often the most important data a new product team ever receives.

**Stage 8 — Commercialization.** Full launch. Production at scale, full marketing investment, distribution rollout. At this stage, the capital at risk is at its maximum. The discipline of the prior seven stages is what justifies that capital.

The research finding that stands out: products with well-defined and well-executed predevelopment activities — specifically concept testing and business analysis — had a higher success rate than products that skipped those stages. Speed to market matters, but not more than getting the concept right before the engineers start building it.

## How Customers Actually Adopt New Products

Having built the product and launched it, the firm faces a different kind of problem: how do customers go from not knowing the product exists to regularly buying it?

Customers don't adopt new products instantaneously. They move through stages. And different customers move at different speeds.

The adoption process has five stages for each individual customer:

**Awareness** — the customer learns the product exists. Marketing's first job. This is where the Dollar Shave Club video did its work. Ninety seconds, massive distribution via social sharing, a memorable message.

**Interest** — the customer wants to know more. The product website does its job here. So do reviews, recommendations, and any content that answers "how does this actually work and is it right for me?"

**Evaluation** — the customer compares against alternatives. This is the stage where Amazon reviews, social media, and word-of-mouth do the heaviest lifting. The customer is asking: "Is this better than what I'm currently doing?" For Dollar Shave Club, the evaluation was easy — the comparison was to paying $20 for a pack of Gillette cartridges.

**Trial** — the customer tests the product with minimal commitment. Subscriptions lower trial cost. Free samples lower it further. The trial design is a marketing decision with significant consequences; a high trial barrier (you can only buy in bulk) will lose customers at this stage who would have converted if trial were cheaper.

**Adoption** — the customer commits to ongoing use. Not a single purchase, but a pattern of repeat purchase that indicates the product has replaced whatever alternative they were using before.

The second framework — Rogers' diffusion of innovations theory — explains why customers within a population adopt at different speeds. Rogers classified adopters into five categories with corresponding population percentages that are roughly consistent across categories and time:

**Innovators (~2.5%)** — adopt first, motivated by novelty and a high tolerance for risk. They'll buy the product before it's proven because trying new things is part of their identity.

**Early adopters (~13.5%)** — adopt second, after they've seen the innovators try it. These are often opinion leaders in their social circles — people others watch for signals about which new things are worth attention.

**Early majority (~34%)** — pragmatic adopters who wait for proof. They want evidence that the product works before they commit. The early majority's proof comes from the early adopters who have already adopted.

**Late majority (~34%)** — skeptical. They adopt under social or economic pressure, not enthusiasm. When the early majority is clearly using something and the holdout starts to feel out of step, the late majority moves.

**Laggards (~16%)** — last to adopt, if they adopt at all. Often defined by their attachment to existing habits and their resistance to anything that asks them to change.

The marketing implication of this sequence is specific and frequently violated: you cannot target the early majority before the early adopters have adopted. The early majority looks for social proof, and the social proof comes from the people who adopted before them. If you've skipped the innovator and early-adopter targeting — if you've spent your early launch marketing dollars on mass-market awareness — you've asked the early majority to adopt without giving them the evidence they require.

Dollar Shave Club got the sequence right. The video launched on YouTube, not on network television. It spread through exactly the channels where innovators and early adopters in the male grooming category were paying attention — social media, tech forums, the parts of the internet where self-aware millennial men talked about brands that were disrupting established industries. Those early adopters, having adopted, became vocal advocates. Their advocacy gave the early majority its proof. The early majority's adoption followed.

## What Makes New Products Succeed

The research finding from the source is worth stating precisely: innovative products that offer unique benefits versus the competition have **five times the success rate** of products with fewer elements of differentiation.

Five times. Not slightly higher. Not meaningfully higher. Five times.

This number suggests that most of the decision-relevant information about a new product's probability of success is contained in a single question: does this product offer something genuinely different from what's already available, that customers actually value?

The Shark Tank numbers tell a version of the same story. Squatty Potty — over $200 million in sales. Bombas Socks — over $225 million. Scrub Daddy — over $330 million. Each of these succeeded because it solved a real problem in a way customers hadn't experienced before. Squatty Potty addressed something the category had completely ignored. Bombas built a premium-and-charitable-giving model into a commodity product. Scrub Daddy had a sponge material that changed texture with water temperature — something that looked like a gimmick until you used it.

The pattern is consistent: products with genuine, perceptible, valued differentiation succeed. Products that are slightly better versions of something already available, without a clear reason for the customer to change their behavior, fail much more often.

The other success factors are largely structural:

**Predevelopment planning** — defining the target market, customer needs, and product requirements before development begins. Teams that know who they're building for make better product decisions than teams building for a notional average customer.

**Speed to market** — in competitive markets, being first matters. A product that reaches the market in time to establish category leadership before a competitor copies it converts early-mover advantage into durable market share.

**Alignment between marketing and R&D** — products developed in isolation from market context fail at higher rates than products whose development team has continuous contact with actual customers and with the marketing team that will eventually have to sell the thing.

## Intellectual Property: Protecting What You Build, Respecting What Others Built

New product development creates IP and creates IP risk simultaneously. The discipline of managing both is a routine part of NPD execution that many firms treat as an afterthought until it becomes a crisis.

Three categories:

**Patents** exclude others from making, using, or selling an invention for a defined period — typically twenty years from filing date in the US. A patent application requires that the invention be novel, non-obvious, and useful. Patents are expensive to obtain and expensive to defend. In March 2021, a jury ordered Apple to pay over $300 million to Personalized Media Communications LLC for patent infringement. In 2021, a judge ruled that Google had infringed on five patents owned by Sonos — a ruling that, if upheld, could have banned imports of Google Home devices and Pixel smartphones.

**Trademarks** protect the words, symbols, and brand identifiers that distinguish one firm's products from another's. Takeda Pharmaceuticals had to rename its antidepressant from Brintellix to Trintellix because Brintellix was confusable with Brilinta, an anti-blood-clotting drug. The confusion risk in a pharmaceutical context isn't just a marketing problem; it's a patient safety problem. The rename cost more than the trademark search that would have caught the conflict in the first place.

**Copyrights** protect original works of authorship — software, songs, films, written content. For product development teams, copyright is most relevant in product documentation, software features, and marketing materials.

Three due-care practices that should be standard in every NPD process:

**Trademark search** before naming the product. Verify the proposed name is clear — not just identical matches but confusable ones. The standard for trademark infringement is "likelihood of confusion," not exact duplication.

**Trademark application filing** as soon as the firm has a bona fide intent to use the name commercially. Filing establishes priority. The six-month window between filing and required commercial use gives the firm time to complete development.

**Freedom-to-operate analysis** — a review of existing patents in the relevant space to verify the new product doesn't practice a patented invention. This is distinct from the firm's own patent filing; freedom-to-operate asks "can we legally sell this?" even if we haven't patented it ourselves.

The cost of these three steps is modest relative to the cost of discovering, at or after launch, that a product can't be legally sold. IP litigation is expensive, slow, and distracting in ways that kill launch momentum even when the firm eventually wins.

## Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 12 introduced the product life cycle — the pattern of introduction, growth, maturity, and decline that most products follow. This chapter is about what happens before the introduction stage: the development process that determines which ideas reach market, in what form, and with what probability of success.

The NPD process is also an application of the marketing research scaffolding from Chapter 8. Concept testing is a research activity. Market testing is a research activity. The seven-step research process applies inside the seven-stage NPD process, and the same discipline — define the research question, choose the right method, design sampling, gather and analyze data, use the findings — applies to both.

The Rogers adopter categories connect to segmentation and targeting from Chapter 7. Innovators and early adopters are a segment. Their specific characteristics — risk tolerance, social influence, category engagement — make them a targetable, reachable, actionable group that the ADAMS criteria would approve. The early majority is a different segment, with different media habits and different proof requirements, and should be targeted only after the earlier adopters have generated the evidence the early majority requires.

Chapter 15 — Pricing — will address how to price new products at launch, which depends on which adopter category the firm is targeting first and what pricing strategy (penetration or skimming) the product's competitive position supports.
