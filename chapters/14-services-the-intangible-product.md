# Chapter 14 — Services: The Intangible Product

*A service is not a product the customer can take home. The marketing implications cascade from there.*

Here is a contrast worth sitting with.

A coffee shop in Tennessee opens near a fast-growing community. The owner, Lisa, builds something distinct from the national chains around her — no drive-through, Wi-Fi throughout, seating designed for working or meeting, locally baked goods, staff who know regulars by name. She calls it Wired Coffee Bar. It does well.

Then drive-through coffee competitors open nearby.

The competitive question becomes stark: does Wired's value justify its premium relative to drive-through speed? The answer depends entirely on the customer. For commuters who want caffeine at 7am with the windows down, the drive-through wins regardless of what Wired does. For regulars who treat the cafe as their morning office or their social third place, Wired wins decisively — because what they're buying isn't coffee. It's a place to be.

The same product (coffee) produced by two different models (drive-through versus third-place cafe) creates two different marketing problems. That is the nature of services. The *form of the service* shapes the marketing strategy more than the underlying service does. And services have structural properties — four of them — that make their marketing fundamentally different from the marketing of physical products.

---

## Four Properties That Change Everything

Before the frameworks, the four properties. They are not independent observations about services. They are a connected set of facts that generate most of the interesting marketing problems in the chapter.

**Intangibility.** A service can't be touched, owned, or stored. A customer can't examine a haircut before getting one. They can't take a massage home to evaluate it later. This creates a specific problem: without a physical product to inspect, customers need proxy signals to judge quality before they buy. The reception area of a law firm. The cleanliness of a doctor's office. The design of a software interface. The staff's appearance and demeanor. None of these is the service — but all of them are evidence the service will be good. Marketing a service means managing those proxy signals deliberately.

**Inseparability.** Services are usually produced and consumed at the same time, with the customer present. You can't get a haircut while the barber is in another building. The service exists only while it's being delivered, and the customer is part of that delivery. The implication is stark: the interaction between the service employee and the customer is not a customer-service moment. It is the product itself. A bad interaction isn't a defect that happens after the sale — it is a product defect during the sale, and it cannot be recalled or repaired after the customer leaves.

**Variability.** Because humans deliver most services, and humans vary, service quality varies. The same hotel restaurant produces a great meal Tuesday and a mediocre one Wednesday. The same barber gives you a better haircut when they're rested than when they're rushed. The firm can reduce variability through training, standardization, and process design — and should — but it cannot eliminate it entirely. Brands that invest most heavily in reducing variability (Ritz-Carlton, Disney, McDonald's) are essentially selling consistency itself as their core product. The consistency is what the premium buys.

**Perishability.** A service that goes unsold today cannot be stored and sold tomorrow. An empty airline seat at departure time is permanent revenue loss. An empty hotel room tonight is gone. A barber chair unoccupied at 2pm cannot be refilled retroactively. This creates demand-management problems that physical products don't have: yield management, dynamic pricing, off-peak promotions, capacity planning. A firm that hasn't thought about perishability is leaving revenue on the table during peak periods and suffering unnecessary losses during slow ones.

All four properties arise from the same underlying fact: a service is an activity, not an object. You can't inventory an activity. You can't ship it. You can't inspect it before the customer receives it. Marketing frameworks built for physical products — ones that assume you can separate production from consumption, inspect quality before delivery, and store excess inventory — break when applied to services. The chapter's frameworks are replacements, not extensions.

---

## Two Service Categories

Services also split into two broad categories based on what delivers them.

**People-based services** are delivered by humans. Three tiers: unskilled labor (parking attendants, janitors, babysitters), skilled labor (plumbers, hairstylists, caterers), and professionals (doctors, attorneys, accountants, college professors). The tier matters for pricing, training investment, brand positioning, and regulatory environment. A professional service carries licensing requirements a skilled-labor service doesn't. A professional's reputation is a primary marketing asset in a way a parking attendant's isn't.

**Equipment-based services** are delivered by machines or technology: ATMs, automated car washes, vending machines, most of cloud software. Different marketing problems — the customer relationship is with the interface, not a person, so variability is lower but the intangibility and perishability challenges remain.

Sara Bullock's case shows how the same fundamental service produces different marketing problems based on form. She ran conventional childcare in Maryland for a decade — monthly commitments, predictable relationships. When her family moved to Tennessee, she opened MeTime Drop-In Child Care: parents can drop children for one to seven hours as needed. Same underlying service (childcare), completely different customer segment (parents who need flexibility), different pricing (per-hour rather than monthly), different value proposition (time freedom for the parent, not just supervision for the child). The business has been successful enough that she's planning a second location. The point isn't the innovation — it's that changing the form of a people-based service changes every marketing variable.

## The Service-Profit Chain

Here is a pattern that shows up in service business failures.

A hotel's guest satisfaction scores drop. Investigation: the front-desk staff are curt, response times are slow, problems aren't resolved on the first contact. Standard diagnosis: customer service failure. Standard fix: customer-service training.

It doesn't work. Scores stay flat. Why?

Because the customer-facing behavior was a symptom. The cause was upstream: front-desk staff are understaffed, equipment breaks regularly, management ignores complaints, the scheduling creates chronic fatigue. Unhappy employees under chronic stress produce inconsistent service. The training teaches scripts to employees who are too depleted to apply them.

A group of Harvard researchers in the 1990s formalized this observation into the service-profit chain. The sequence:

1. **Internal service quality** — the support frontline employees receive from the organization: working equipment, clear policies, proper training, fair compensation, management that listens.
2. → **Employee satisfaction** — employees who feel supported are more engaged.
3. → **Employee loyalty and productivity** — engaged employees stay longer, work harder, and go beyond the minimum.
4. → **Value creation** — employees who go beyond the minimum produce experiences customers genuinely value.
5. → **Customer satisfaction** — value drives satisfaction.
6. → **Customer loyalty** — satisfied customers come back.
7. → **Profitability and growth** — loyal customers, lower acquisition costs, premium pricing power.

The chain is sequential. Skipping links breaks downstream outcomes. A firm that tries to drive profitability without first addressing internal service quality pushes frontline employees into behaviors — forced smiling, scripted cheerfulness under exhaustion — that customers read as hollow and that employees find unsustainable. Both groups leave.

Restaurants make the metaphor concrete. **Front-of-house** staff (hosts, servers) interact with customers. **Back-of-house** staff (cooks, dishwashers, line prep) support the interaction without direct customer contact. The customer experience depends on both. A warm and attentive server is undermined by dirty silverware or a 40-minute wait for food that was mis-fired in the kitchen. Internal service quality means the back-of-house has what the front-of-house needs.

The Services Marketing Triangle organizes the same relationships geometrically. Three points: the firm, its employees, and its customers. Three types of marketing connect them:

- **External marketing** (firm → customer) sets the brand promise: what the customer is told to expect.
- **Internal marketing** (firm → employee) enables that promise: ensuring employees understand the brand, have the tools to deliver it, and feel invested in its success.
- **Interactive marketing** (employee → customer) delivers the promise: the actual moment of service.

All three must align. External marketing that promises a level of service the firm hasn't enabled internally produces Gap 4 (below). Internal marketing that invests in employees without a clear external promise produces confused brand positioning. Interactive marketing without internal support produces inconsistent delivery regardless of how well-trained the employee is.

## Five Places Service Quality Fails

The Gap Model of Service Quality, proposed in 1985, identifies five specific places where the gap between what the customer expects and what the customer perceives can open. The model is diagnostic: it doesn't tell you service quality failed — it tells you *where* it failed, which determines what fix will actually work.

**Gap 1 — Knowledge gap.** Management's understanding of what customers expect differs from what customers actually expect. A hotel manager assumes guests want a hot breakfast; guests care far more about reliable Wi-Fi and quiet. The cause: insufficient market research, weak customer feedback loops, executives disconnected from customer experience. The fix is research — specifically the kind described in Chapter 8, designed to capture real needs rather than assumed ones.

**Gap 2 — Policy gap.** Management understands what customers need but fails to translate that understanding into specific service standards. "Answer the phone quickly" is not a standard. "Answer within three rings" is. The distance between knowing what customers want and specifying how employees should deliver it is Gap 2. It lives in the policy documents (or their absence).

**Gap 3 — Delivery gap.** Standards exist but employees don't meet them. The policy says three rings; the average is seven. The cause is almost always operational: understaffing, inadequate training, equipment failures, employee burnout. This is where the service-profit chain intersects the gap model — a gap 3 problem is usually a gap in internal service quality, not a gap in employee intention.

**Gap 4 — Communication gap.** Marketing communicates something the service doesn't deliver. The brochure promises 24-hour room service; the hotel actually offers it 6am–10pm. The campaign says "the fastest checkout in retail"; the actual checkout is average. The gap opens when marketing is written by people who aren't constrained by operational reality. It closes when marketing and operations share the same information about what the service can actually deliver.

**Gap 5 — Customer gap.** The customer perceives less than they expected. This is the gap the customer experiences directly — the accumulated effect of any of Gaps 1 through 4 that weren't caught earlier. The Gap 5 score is what shows up in customer satisfaction surveys; the other four gaps are what caused it.

Corporate Medical Services — a Tennessee-based drug and alcohol testing firm serving the trucking industry — demonstrates the model applied to prevention. Their value proposition maps explicitly onto gap-closing commitments: *"unrivaled experience"* (Gap 1: they've served 15,000+ companies over 25+ years, so management genuinely understands what customers need); *"concierge-level personal service"* (Gap 2: the language is specific enough to translate into behavioral standards); *"developed processes, procedures, performance benchmarks for compliance"* (Gap 3: operational infrastructure that makes delivery consistent); *"7-minute turnaround on negative reports once released from the lab"* (Gap 4: a specific, measurable promise that marketing can make because operations can keep it).

The pattern: each external promise is paired with an operational commitment that closes one of the upstream gaps. Promises without operational backing produce gap failures. Operational excellence without promises wastes the marketing leverage.

## The 7Ps: Three More Levers for Services

The standard 4Ps — Product, Price, Place, Promotion — were designed for physical products. They're necessary for services but not sufficient. The four service characteristics require three additional levers:

**People.** The front-of-house employees who deliver the service are not a background variable — they are part of the product. The service-profit chain makes this concrete: the quality of the people delivering the service is a marketing decision, not just an HR decision. Hiring standards, training investment, compensation, culture — all of these shape what the customer experiences.

**Process.** The workflows that produce the service consistently are a marketing lever. Standardized processes reduce variability. McDonald's burger assembly process is not primarily an operations decision — it's a marketing decision that delivers the brand promise of consistency across 40,000 locations. The process is the mechanism that makes the intangible tangible.

**Physical evidence.** Because services are intangible, tangible cues that signal quality become disproportionately important. The reception area. The website design. The uniform. The coffee shop's lighting and seating. The SaaS onboarding email. These are all proxy signals that help the customer evaluate a service they can't yet touch. Managing physical evidence is managing the customer's expectation before the service begins.

The 7Ps framework isn't a replacement for the 4Ps. It's the 4Ps plus three additional levers that service businesses need to pull deliberately. Product, Price, Place, and Promotion are still live decisions. People, Process, and Physical Evidence are the mechanisms through which the first four commitments get delivered or fail to.

## What the Chapter Leaves You With

The US economy is primarily a service economy. Service industries contributed roughly 67 percent of US GDP in 2020, versus 10.8 percent for manufacturing. More than half of the US workforce produces intangibles. A marketing education that covers only physical products is a marketing education that misses most of the modern economy.

The four service characteristics — intangibility, inseparability, variability, perishability — are not complications layered onto product marketing. They are the fundamental facts that generate a different set of marketing problems. Once you understand the four properties, the service-profit chain, the gap model, and the 7Ps extension are all logical responses to those problems rather than arbitrary frameworks to memorize.

Here is the test. Pick a service experience you had recently. Where did you experience quality — or a failure of it? Work backwards. Was the failure in what the firm understood about your expectations (Gap 1)? In how that understanding was translated into standards (Gap 2)? In whether the employee met those standards (Gap 3)? In what the marketing had promised versus what was delivered (Gap 4)? Or was it entirely in your own expectations running ahead of what the service could deliver (Gap 5)?

And then — was the root cause an employee problem, or an internal service quality problem the employee inherited?

Most service quality failures that look like employee failures are organizational failures visible at the customer touchpoint. The gap model finds the failure. The service-profit chain finds the cause.

## Exercises

### Warm-up

**1. Identify the property.** For each service failure or challenge, identify which of the four properties (intangibility, inseparability, variability, perishability) is most directly responsible, and name the marketing implication in one sentence.

- A customer can't evaluate the quality of a legal consultation before hiring the attorney.
- A hotel has 40 empty rooms on a Tuesday night that cannot be sold Wednesday.
- The same nail salon produces excellent results one visit and mediocre results two weeks later with a different technician.
- A gym class requires the instructor to be physically present with the students.

*Tests: Objective 3. Difficulty: Low.*

**2. Classify the service.** For each of the following, identify whether it's people-based or equipment-based. If people-based, identify the sub-category (unskilled, skilled, or professional).

- A licensed electrician rewiring a kitchen.
- An ATM dispensing cash.
- A college professor teaching a marketing course.
- A babysitter watching children on a Saturday night.
- A vending machine dispensing a cold drink.

*Tests: Objective 2. Difficulty: Low.*

**3. Name the gap.** For each of the following service quality failures, identify which of the five gaps is most directly at fault. Give one sentence explaining why.

- A dental office marketing promises "minimal wait times"; patients routinely wait 45 minutes past their appointment.
- A restaurant manager believes customers' top priority is food quantity; customers actually care most about consistency and speed.
- A hotel's policy says guest complaints should be resolved within one hour; front-desk staff have no authority to issue refunds and must escalate everything to a manager who is often unavailable.
- A coffee shop's baristas make excellent espresso drinks Monday and mediocre ones Saturday because the experienced staff only work weekdays.

*Tests: Objective 6. Difficulty: Low.*

### Application

**4. Trace the service-profit chain.** A regional airline has high customer satisfaction scores but is struggling with employee turnover — front-line staff (gate agents, flight attendants) leave within 18 months on average. Senior management wants to launch a new customer-experience training program.

Trace this situation through the service-profit chain: (a) identify where the chain is broken, (b) explain why the proposed fix (training) addresses the wrong link, (c) specify one structural change that would address the actual break in the chain, (d) predict what would happen to customer satisfaction scores 12 months after implementing the structural change versus 12 months after the training program.

*Tests: Objective 4. Difficulty: Medium.*

**5. Apply the Services Marketing Triangle.** Choose a service brand you can research — a bank, a healthcare provider, a gym chain, a hotel brand, anything with public marketing materials and enough employee reviews on Glassdoor or similar to give you internal information. Map the triangle: (a) what does external marketing promise the customer, (b) what does internal marketing appear to tell employees (infer from employee reviews and internal communications if public), (c) what do customer reviews suggest actually happens in interactive marketing? Identify any misalignment between the three legs and propose one specific action to close the largest gap.

*Tests: Objective 5. Difficulty: Medium.*

**6. Design the 7Ps for a new service.** A solo therapist is opening a private practice offering in-person and telehealth therapy sessions. Specify all 7Ps for the practice. Pay particular attention to the three service Ps — People, Process, Physical Evidence — and explain for each one how it addresses a specific challenge created by the four service properties.

*Tests: Objective 7. Difficulty: Medium.*

### Synthesis

**7. Run all frameworks on one firm.** Pick a service firm you can investigate — a fast-casual restaurant, a gym, a software-as-a-service product, an airline, anything with public data and customer reviews. Apply: (a) the four properties and how the firm handles each, (b) the service-profit chain — trace each link from internal service quality through to profitability and identify the weakest link, (c) the gap model — which of the five gaps is most visibly active based on customer reviews or public complaints? For the most active gap, trace it to its operational source and propose a structural fix (not a training program, not a script).

*Tests: Objectives 3, 4, 6. Difficulty: Medium-high.*

**8. Connect to Chapter 12.** Peloton, from Chapter 12, sells a physical product bundled with a substantial service (streaming subscription, community, coaching). Apply the four service properties to Peloton's service component: (a) how does Peloton handle intangibility in marketing the subscription, (b) how does inseparability shape the customer's experience of the live classes, (c) where does variability appear in the service component, and what does Peloton do to reduce it, (d) how does perishability affect live class capacity, and what does Peloton do about it? Then apply the 7Ps to the full Peloton bundle — which of the three service Ps (People, Process, Physical Evidence) is most central to Peloton's $B$ in the value ratio, and why?

*Tests: Objectives 1, 3, 7. Difficulty: Medium-high.*

### Challenge

**9. The structural-fix audit.** Find a service firm whose customer satisfaction problems have been publicly documented — through news coverage, customer review data, government complaints, or published case studies. Diagnose the active gap or gaps using the model. For each active gap, propose a structural fix — not a training program, not a script, not a customer-service department expansion. A structural fix changes the policies, processes, staffing ratios, incentive structures, or organizational design that generated the gap. For each fix, estimate what it would cost and what the downstream effect on Gap 5 (the customer gap) would be. No fabrication — use documented evidence and label inferences explicitly.

*Tests: Objectives 4, 6, 8. Difficulty: High.*
