# Chapter 10 — Marketing in a Diverse Marketplace

*A domestic market is not a single market. The discipline of marketing across cultural, ethnic, generational, and identity differences within one country is harder than international marketing in some ways — because the boundaries are invisible.*

Here is a single-day disaster worth understanding.

In early 2018, Tarte Cosmetics launched its Shape Tape Foundation. It was a highly anticipated product — the brand had a loyal following and the launch was loud. The product came in 15 shades. Almost all of them were light.

Within hours, loyal customers with darker skin tones were furious. The accusations were direct: whitewashed brand, making darker-skinned customers feel excluded. Tarte apologized, changed the product, revised its communication.

The quick read on this story: a company made a bad marketing decision and corrected it. The correct read is different. The failure didn't happen at the marketing stage. It happened in product development, when the people deciding which shades to include didn't include enough perspectives to catch what they were missing. By the time the marketing launched, the structural error was already locked in. A faster apology process wouldn't have prevented it. Only different representation in the development pipeline would have.

That is the insight this chapter is built on. Diversity marketing isn't a final-stage creative adjustment. It is a structural discipline — present or absent well before the campaign runs.

---

## What Diversity Marketing Actually Is

From Chapter 9: **standardization** means the same marketing strategy in all markets. **Adaptation** means adjusting strategy for different markets. Both options exist within a single country.

Diversity marketing is adaptation strategy applied to different cultural and sociodemographic segments within one domestic market. The marketer adjusts product, price, place, or promotion for each segment's preferences — not because the segments are in different countries, but because they are meaningfully different in ways that affect what they value, how they decide, and what resonates.

The US makes this discipline urgent. The Census Bureau projects the United States will become a **plurality nation** by approximately 2050 — no single demographic group will hold a majority share of the population. The non-Hispanic White population will remain the largest single group but will lose its majority position. A national campaign aimed at "the average American consumer" describes a person who is becoming statistically rarer every year.

This is not only an ethical argument, though the ethical argument stands. It is a strategic one. Adobe's 2019 survey of 2,000+ consumers found 61 percent believed diversity in marketing campaigns was important, and 38 percent were more inclined to trust brands that showed genuine diversity. Heat agency analysis found high diversity scores translated to 83 percent higher consumer preference. Top Design Firms found 67 percent of consumers would consider repeat purchases from companies committed to diversity in marketing.

The trade-off is between adaptation cost (multiple campaigns, additional SKUs, supply-chain complexity) and inclusion benefit (trust, preference, access to growth segments worth hundreds of billions of dollars in historically unmet demand). Standardization saves cost. Adaptation increases relevance. The inclusion benefit has been historically underestimated, and the data is starting to show by how much.

The failure mode on the cost side of the trade-off is not just wasted budget. It is the specific damage done by campaigns that look like diversity marketing but aren't — campaigns that perform representation for an outside audience rather than actually serving the inside audience. The term for this is tokenism. Consumers have become better at detecting it, particularly younger consumers who have grown up surrounded by marketing claims and developed sharper instincts for distinguishing authentic engagement from performance.

P&G's arc is the useful contrast. In 1961, P&G aired one of the first major US television commercials in Spanish on a Spanish-language channel that later became Univisión. In 1965, it launched affirmative action plans across all business units. The same year, it conducted product research with Hispanic consumers in Miami on its Secret deodorant scent. Sixty years later, P&G's Chief Brand Officer Marc Pritchard described a commitment that extends to equitable representation throughout the entire supply chain — not just in ads, but in the subcontracted agencies, production crews, and media companies doing the work. The supply-chain inclusion is the structural discipline. The campaigns are downstream of it.

## The Iceberg Below the Waterline

Before the specific segments, a framework for understanding where cultural friction comes from.

Anthropologist Edward T. Hall described culture as an iceberg. About 10 percent is above the waterline — visible, recognizable, easy to reproduce. Traditions, music, language, clothing, food, artifacts. This is the part most marketers reach for first. The other 90 percent is below: thought processes, assumptions, values, beliefs, interpretations, worldviews. Hard to observe. The source of most marketing failures.

The Burger King "Texican Whopper" campaign featured a tiny Mexican wrestler in a poncho-style Mexican flag standing next to a tall American cowboy. Some Spanish-speaking audiences found it funny. Others were offended — both by the stereotyping and by the use of the flag as costume. In Mexico and many Latin American countries, the national flag commands deep respect as a symbol of sovereignty and national identity. Using it as clothing treats a national symbol as decoration. The campaign team had engaged the visible 10 percent — they used a Mexican character, a Mexican visual reference — without understanding what that symbol meant beneath the surface. The campaign had to be revised.

The iceberg model explains why **translating a campaign into Spanish is not the same as marketing to Hispanic audiences**. Translation handles the tangible surface. The intangible layer — cultural practices without clean translations, values embedded in ways of living, references that carry specific meaning within a community — requires something else.

Target's Sobremesa / #SinTraducción campaign demonstrates what below-the-waterline engagement looks like. The *sobremesa* is the Hispanic tradition of extended time at the dinner table after a meal — relaxing, continuing conversation, staying together. The word has no direct English equivalent. Target's tagline: *"There will always be a part of you that simply doesn't translate."* The campaign acknowledged a specific cultural practice rather than adding Spanish words to standard messaging. It worked because it engaged something real below the surface — a shared experience that didn't require translation because the people it was addressing already understood it from the inside.

## Three Multicultural Segments

The US multicultural market is not a single segment. It is a collection of communities with distinct histories, identities, and preferences, most of which are themselves internally diverse. Three are large enough and fast-growing enough to examine specifically.

**Hispanic identity.** The 2020 US Census counted 63+ million Hispanic people — 1 in 5 Americans — representing 23 percent growth since the previous census. Projections estimate roughly 100 million by 2050. The Hispanic population spans 20 different countries of origin. Five subethnicities account for 83 percent: Mexican (62.2%), Puerto Rican (9.5%), Cuban (3.9%), Salvadoran (3.9%), Dominican (3.5%). The remaining 17 percent distributes across 15 others.

Three terms are used interchangeably but mean different things. **Hispanic** is language-based: people from Spanish-speaking countries. A person from Spain is Hispanic but not Latino. **Latino/Latina** is location-based: people whose families originate in Latin America regardless of language. A person from Brazil is Latina but not Hispanic. **Latinx** is a gender-neutral form that many Hispanic people dislike or find offensive, despite its adoption in some communities. The vocabulary is contested — using it without understanding that contest is itself a below-the-waterline error.

**Black identity.** The 2020 Census showed 46.8 million Black people — 14.2 percent of the population. Black identification "in combination with another race" increased 88.7 percent from the prior census, the largest growth among racial categories. McKinsey estimates Black consumers will represent 15–17 percent of total US population growth over the next decade, with over $300 billion per year in currently unmet demand. A 2019 Pew Research Center survey found that nearly 75 percent of Black people believe race is at the center of their identity. Effective marketing to Black consumers acknowledges this — not by foregrounding race in every campaign, but by ensuring the campaign reflects authentic Black perspectives in its development and production. P&G's "Widen the Screen," debuted during the 2021 NAACP Image Awards, featured short episodes by Black creators challenging stereotypes. Oprah Winfrey noted that Black creators account for only 6 percent of all films made. The campaign's argument was structural: the underrepresentation in marketing reflects underrepresentation in the industry that produces it.

**Asian identity.** The Asian American community comprises 22 countries of origin with distinct cultural, linguistic, and historical contexts. Color symbolism illustrates why pan-Asian assumptions fail: red is lucky for many Chinese consumers; green symbolizes youth and vitality in Japanese culture; yellow is associated with vitality and light in Korean culture. A campaign that foregrounds red to signal "Asian-friendly" is assuming Chinese cultural preferences extend to Japanese and Korean audiences — which they don't. The assumption is stereotyping wearing diversity-imagery cover.

The concept of **acculturation** applies across all three segments. Acculturation describes how individuals from multicultural backgrounds adapt to and integrate dominant cultural practices while maintaining their cultural identity. A second-generation Mexican-American consumer may speak English at home and Spanish with extended family, prefer one set of media in some contexts and a different set in others, and respond to different marketing approaches at different moments. Acculturation is a continuum, not a binary — and it differs by generation, geography, family context, and individual choice. Marketing that treats any multicultural group as at a single point on this continuum will be wrong for most of them.

## Sociodemographic Segments

Beyond ethnicity and race, marketers target groups defined by other identity dimensions: gender and sexual identity, generational cohort, religion, disability.

**LGBTQIA+ consumers.** The acronym stands for lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender, queer/questioning, intersex, and asexual, with the + signaling other sexual identities and allies. In 2012, 3.5 percent of Americans self-identified as LGBTQIA+. By 2020 (Gallup), the figure was 5.6 percent. One in six adults in Generation Z — born 1997 to 2012 — identifies as LGBTQIA+, suggesting the share will continue rising as older cohorts age out and younger ones enter adulthood. The operational guidance for marketing to this community: review company values honestly (is the support genuine or performative?), establish guidelines on language and imagery, involve community members in the work, and test whether the marketing is perceived as it was designed to be. IKEA's #ProgressIsMade campaign during 2021 US Pride Month generated up to $50,000 in proceeds donated to GLSEN, an LGBTQIA+ nonprofit focused on education. In Canada, IKEA displayed 10 concept-art love seats inspired by Pride flags at select stores. The campaign worked because the commercial activity was paired with sustained commitment — not because it added rainbows to standard marketing.

**Generational cohorts.** The standard segmentation: Silent Generation (~1928–1945), Baby Boomers (1946–1964), Generation X (1965–1980), Millennials (1981–1996), Generation Z (1997–2012), Generation Alpha (2010–2025). A Generation Alpha child is born every nine seconds in the United States; the cohort is projected to reach 2 billion individuals worldwide by 2025. Cohort marketing is useful at the macro level and dangerous at the individual level. The most consistent failure mode: over-applying cohort generalizations to individuals. Baby Boomers are active radio listeners — a marketer who assumes they're not and excludes them from a radio buy is making a structural error based on a demographic stereotype, not evidence.

**Consumers with disabilities.** The ADA and related laws govern accessibility and marketing practices toward consumers with disabilities. Marketing implications: accessibility in advertising channels (captions on video, alt text on images, accessible website design), authentic representation (including consumers with disabilities in product development and creative), and avoiding "inspiration porn" — marketing that frames disability primarily as a source of able-bodied inspiration rather than as a normal part of human experience. The disability community is large, represents significant purchasing power, and is systematically underrepresented in marketing.

The trade-off across all sociodemographic targeting is the same as in multicultural marketing: segmentation precision versus stereotype risk. The same tool that helps marketers reach historically excluded groups efficiently also helps them stereotype efficiently. The fix isn't to abandon sociodemographic targeting — that would re-exclude the groups it can reach effectively. The fix is to do it research-grounded rather than assumption-grounded, with real representation in development rather than symbolic representation in the final creative.

## Four Ethics Risks

Every framework in this chapter is also a failure mode if misapplied. Four risks are worth naming explicitly.

**Stereotypes and unconscious bias.** Marketing campaigns reinforce or challenge cultural and social stereotypes. Stereotypes are efficient — a 30-second ad can communicate character and context quickly by reaching for stock assumptions. The cost: those assumptions limit how the audience perceives the represented group, and they compound over time. P&G's 2014 "Like a Girl" campaign challenged the use of "like a girl" as a put-down, naming the stereotype explicitly and transforming it. That naming is what made it work. A campaign that deploys a stereotype without naming it reinforces it. A campaign made by a team whose assumptions are never examined produces the team's assumptions as output — which usually means the dominant culture's assumptions, invisible because they're unexamined.

**Tokenism.** Including individuals from a minority group to prevent criticism without genuinely engaging with their perspectives, needs, or representation. Tokenism looks like representation but doesn't deliver it. The test: would the audience itself recognize the campaign as serving them, or as performing for outsiders watching? Consumers have better instincts for this distinction than most marketers assume — particularly younger consumers who have grown up surrounded by marketing claims.

**Cultural and language errors.** There were 7,139 living languages reported worldwide in 2021. Twenty-three of those account for the majority of global speakers. The category of error runs from direct translation that misses idiom — "9Lives" cat food works in English (cats have nine lives); in Spanish and Portuguese idiom, cats have *seven* lives — to symbol misuse. The rainbow flag means "LGBTQIA+ community" in the US; it also resembles the Andean rainbow flag that symbolizes the Inca empire to Peruvian and Andean communities. Using one without distinguishing it from the other produces unintended cross-cultural meaning. Color symbolism errors (red as luck in Chinese culture, green as youth in Japanese culture, yellow as vitality in Korean culture) produce pan-Asian assumptions that don't hold across the 22 countries the Asian American community spans.

**The ethics-business connection.** All four risks damage the value ratio. $V = B/P$ is computed in the customer's head, and customers from diverse backgrounds increasingly factor the firm's authentic engagement with their community into the $B$ side of the ratio. Tokenism reduces $B$. Genuine engagement increases it. The data from earlier in this chapter — 67 percent willing to make repeat purchases from diversity-committed brands, 83 percent higher consumer preference for high-diversity-score brands — is consumer-side evidence that the ethics failure has a financial cost, and the ethics investment has a financial return.

## The Standard the Chapter Leaves You With

P&G's six-decade arc — from a Spanish-language TV ad in 1961 to supply-chain inclusion standards in 2021 — is useful precisely because it shows the work as cumulative rather than episodic. Diversity marketing that survives contact with a real diverse audience is not the result of a well-intended campaign. It is the result of structurally inclusive practices in product development, creative production, and media selection that precede the campaign by months or years.

The Tarte Cosmetics story is the counter-case. The failure was upstream of marketing. The fix is also upstream of marketing: diverse representation in the development pipeline before the product locks. By the time the launch runs, the structural error is already baked in.

The discipline this chapter leaves you with is a set of questions to ask before a campaign runs — not after. Whose perspectives were in the room when the product was developed? Whose perspectives were in the room when the creative was approved? Does the campaign engage the intangible layer of the audience's culture, or only the visible surface? Would the audience itself recognize this as serving them?

If you can answer those questions with evidence rather than assumption, you are doing diversity marketing. If you're answering them with confidence and no evidence, you are probably doing the other thing.

## Exercises

### Warm-up

**1. Above or below the waterline.** Using Hall's iceberg model, classify each of the following as tangible (above) or intangible (below) cultural elements, and explain in one sentence why the classification matters for a marketer.

- A traditional dish served at family gatherings.
- The belief that direct disagreement with an elder is disrespectful.
- The expectation that a brand's customer service will respond within 24 hours.
- A national flag used as decoration in a product image.
- The assumption that silence during a negotiation signals rejection rather than consideration.

*Tests: Objective 4. Difficulty: Low.*

**2. Multicultural vocabulary check.** For each statement, identify whether it is accurate, inaccurate, or context-dependent, and explain your reasoning in one sentence.

- "Hispanic and Latino mean the same thing."
- "A person from Brazil is Latino but not Hispanic."
- "Latinx is the preferred term among Hispanic communities."
- "All Asian American consumers share similar cultural values around color symbolism."

*Tests: Objective 5. Difficulty: Low.*

**3. Identify the segment type.** For each of the following campaigns, identify the primary diversity parameter being engaged — cultural/racial/ethnic, gender/sexual identity, generational, or disability — and whether the campaign appears to operate above or below the cultural waterline.

- A bank targets Baby Boomers with messaging about retirement security.
- A cosmetics brand launches a product line with 40 foundation shades across skin tones.
- A tech company releases a Pride Month social post with a rainbow logo but no other commitment.
- A grocery chain redesigns its store layout to improve navigation for customers with visual impairments.

*Tests: Objectives 3, 4. Difficulty: Low.*

### Application

**4. Apply the tokenism test.** The chapter frames a five-question test for tokenism: Does the brand's product genuinely serve this audience? Did the brand consult community members in development? Does the campaign treat identity as a feature of the audience or as a feature of the brand's marketing? Would the audience itself recognize the campaign as serving them? Is the worst-case interpretation defensible?

Apply all five questions to one of the following campaigns. For each question, write two to three sentences with your assessment. Conclude with a verdict — substantive engagement or tokenism — and explain what specifically would have to change to move it from one to the other.

- Pepsi's 2017 Kendall Jenner ad.
- A major bank that runs Black History Month social media content but has no Black board members or senior executives.
- A fast-food brand that releases a Pride Month cup design while donating to political candidates who oppose LGBTQIA+ rights.

*Tests: Objectives 7, 8. Difficulty: Medium.*

**5. Diagnose the Tarte failure upstream.** The chapter argues the Tarte Shape Tape failure was a product-development failure, not a marketing failure. Identify three specific points in the product development and launch process — before the marketing ran — where the failure could have been caught and corrected. For each point, describe what a diverse-representation standard would have required and what the cost of implementing it would likely have been (time, money, process change).

*Tests: Objectives 1, 7. Difficulty: Medium.*

**6. Design a below-the-waterline campaign.** A US coffee brand wants to market authentically to one of the following segments. Choose one: second-generation Mexican-American young adults, Black professional women aged 28–45, or Korean American college students.

Design the concept for a campaign that engages an intangible cultural element — not language, not visual representation of the demographic, but a specific practice, value, or shared experience that lives below the waterline. Specify: (a) the specific intangible element you are engaging and why it is real (not stereotyped), (b) how you would verify that your understanding of the element is accurate rather than assumed, (c) what the campaign concept is, and (d) how you would test whether the target audience experiences it as serving them.

*Tests: Objectives 4, 5, 6. Difficulty: Medium.*

### Synthesis

**7. Connect acculturation to the value ratio.** The chapter describes acculturation as a continuum — a second-generation Hispanic consumer occupies a different point on it than a first-generation one, and the point shifts by context. Pick one multicultural segment from the chapter. Describe how the $B$ in the value ratio ($V = B/P$) differs for three hypothetical consumers in that segment at different points on the acculturation continuum. What does this imply for a brand that wants to serve all three without treating the segment as monolithic?

*Tests: Objectives 1, 5. Difficulty: Medium-high.*

**8. Audit the supply chain.** P&G's standard — equitable representation throughout the entire supply chain, including subcontracted agencies, production crews, and media companies — is the chapter's benchmark for structural diversity marketing. Pick a brand you can research. Investigate, as far as public information allows: (a) who produces their identity-targeted campaigns (agencies named in press releases, production credits), (b) whether those production partners have disclosed diversity data, (c) what gaps exist between the brand's claimed diversity commitments and the supply chain producing them, (d) one specific change that would close the largest gap. Label clearly what you found in public sources and what you are inferring.

*Tests: Objectives 1, 8. Difficulty: Medium-high.*

### Challenge

**9. Forecast the plurality-nation shift.** The US is projected to become a plurality nation by approximately 2050. Pick a product category currently marketed primarily to a non-Hispanic White majority consumer. Describe what a 25-year marketing evolution should look like as the demographic balance shifts. Specify: (a) which segments will gain share of the relevant consumer population, (b) which current marketing assumptions will become obsolete, (c) what specific changes in product, price, place, or promotion are required to serve the new majority, and (d) what the firm should begin doing in the next three years to avoid arriving at 2040 with a strategy built for a demographic reality that no longer exists. Base your projections on the population data and trends cited in the chapter; label any extrapolations as inference.

*Tests: Objectives 1–3, 5, 6. Difficulty: High.*
