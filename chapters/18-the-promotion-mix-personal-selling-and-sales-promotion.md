# Chapter 18 — The Promotion Mix: Personal Selling and Sales Promotion

*Two more promotion mix elements. Personal selling builds relationships one customer at a time. Sales promotion produces immediate purchase action.*

Here is a sales story worth understanding mechanically.

Hilton Grand Vacations offers loyal Hilton customers a discounted three-night, four-day hotel stay — typically somewhere appealing — in exchange for attending a property tour and sales presentation for time-share ownership. The discount (a sales promotion) brings the customer. The property tour (a presentation) builds desire. The sales meeting (a close) captures the commitment. The salesperson at HGV is typically a licensed real estate agent. The cycle runs continuously and produces recurring revenue that pure advertising could never reach.

What makes the HGV model worth studying isn't that it's clever. It's that it's explicit about something most sales processes leave implicit: a sales promotion and a personal selling interaction are doing different jobs, and both are necessary. The promotion gets the customer to the table. The salesperson closes the deal. Neither does the other's job.

That separation — what sales promotion does versus what personal selling does — is the animating question of this chapter.

---

## What Personal Selling Is, and When It Works

Personal selling is one-on-one interaction between a company representative and a buyer: uncover needs, present how the product meets those needs, build the relationship that converts into a sale.

This sounds like the whole job of marketing, compressed into a single human interaction. In some contexts, it is. In others, it's wildly inefficient. The difference lies in three product characteristics:

**High price.** A salesperson's time costs money. The math only works if the deal is large enough to justify the investment. A car dealership justifies a commissioned salesperson for a $30,000 transaction. A grocery store doesn't justify one for a $3 purchase.

**Complexity.** A product that requires explanation, customization, or technical specification benefits from someone who can answer questions in real time. Cloud enterprise software, medical equipment, and commercial real estate all require salespeople. Canned soup doesn't.

**Customizability.** When the customer's requirements differ enough that one product doesn't fit all, a salesperson who can diagnose the specific need and configure the right solution adds genuine value. A salesperson who simply takes orders for a standardized product adds less.

Most B2B sales (Chapter 6) check all three boxes. Many B2C high-consideration purchases — cars, real estate, financial products — check at least two. The rest of retail runs on mass channels precisely because personal selling's economics don't justify it.

The highest form of personal selling is **relationship selling**: becoming a trusted advisor rather than a transaction processor. TalEx, a fast-growing HR firm, builds long-term relationships with corporate clients through sales reps who consult on talent strategy alongside placing candidates. The relationship produces lifetime customer value that far exceeds any single transaction — and requires patience, depth of knowledge, and genuine curiosity about the customer's situation that pure transactional selling doesn't demand.

## Four Roles, Very Different Jobs

Not all salespeople are doing the same thing. Four roles are worth distinguishing because each requires different skills, different training, and different management.

**Inside order takers** handle inbound inquiries — phone calls, online orders, walk-in customers. They process purchases that customers have largely already decided to make. The skill set emphasizes accuracy, friendliness, and speed. The restaurant that orders supplies from a distributor by phone is dealing with an inside order taker.

**Outside order takers** visit customer locations to collect routine orders. Common in consumer packaged goods distribution and some industrial supplies. The route sales rep who stops by a convenience store weekly to check inventory and place a reorder is an outside order taker. The interaction is relational — the customer knows the rep — but the rep isn't selling in the persuasive sense. They're maintaining an existing relationship.

**Order getters** actively pursue new business. They prospect for leads, pitch products, handle objections, and close deals. This is the role most people picture when they picture "a salesperson." Order getters are the most expensive per hour, the hardest to manage, and — when they're good — the most valuable. They're the people who build the top of a firm's revenue pipeline from nothing.

**Missionary sellers** build relationships and educate without directly closing sales. The pharmaceutical representative who details doctors on a new medication isn't selling to the doctors — doctors don't buy drugs directly. They're building enough familiarity and trust that doctors will prescribe the medication to patients who do buy it. Industry-analyst relations teams do similar work: cultivating the people who influence buyers without being buyers themselves. The connection to a sale is indirect but real.

Most firms combine multiple role types across their sales organization. An enterprise software company typically has order getters (account executives hunting new customers), inside order takers (handling inbound trials and renewals), customer-success managers (expanding usage in existing accounts), and missionary roles (developer relations or analyst relations). The mix depends on the product, the customer type, and the buying process.

## Seven Steps, In Order

The personal selling process has seven steps. They look like a checklist, but they're actually a dependency chain — skipping a step usually makes later steps fail.

**1. Prospecting and qualifying.** Find potential customers; assess which are worth pursuing. Prospecting methods range from trade shows and referral programs to purchased lists and digital lead generation. Qualifying filters the prospects: does this person have the budget, the authority, the need, and the timeline to actually buy? A large unqualified pipeline is expensive to work and produces poor conversion. A smaller well-qualified pipeline converts efficiently. The hardest part of sales is often Step 1, not Step 6.

**2. Preapproach.** Research the prospect before first contact. Who is in the buying center (Chapter 6)? What are the prospect's specific needs? What are they currently using, and why might they switch? A rep who arrives at the first conversation knowing the prospect's business, industry, and current situation creates a different impression than one who arrives asking "so, tell me about your company."

**3. Approach.** The initial contact. First impressions establish credibility. The approach should be tailored to the buying context — a formal enterprise sales call opens differently from a walk-in retail consultation.

**4. Presentation.** Demonstrate how the product satisfies the prospect's specific needs. Not a generic pitch — a tailored argument that links the product's capabilities to the needs identified in preapproach and the approach conversation. The presentation should include evidence, ideally specific to the prospect's situation.

**5. Handling objections.** Address concerns. Common objections: price, implementation timeline, fit with existing systems, skepticism about claims, inertia toward the status quo. The discipline here is to listen to the objection fully before responding, and to distinguish legitimate concerns (which require real answers) from reflexive resistance (which may just need acknowledgment and patience).

**6. Close.** Convert the presentation into commitment. Multiple closing approaches exist; the right one depends on the context. A soft close works for a prospect who's almost there. A direct close works for a prospect who's decided but needs permission to say yes. A trial close ("would you like to move to the next step?") tests readiness without forcing a binary decision. The common failure is over-closing — pushing harder when the prospect needs more time, which produces resentment rather than commitment.

**7. Follow-up.** Post-sale relationship management. Ensures satisfaction, generates referrals, opens future expansion opportunities. Follow-up is where relationship selling distinguishes itself from transactional selling. A rep who disappears after the close produces one transaction. A rep who stays engaged produces a relationship that generates future transactions and referrals.

## Running a Sales Force

A single rep is a job. A sales force is an organization. Managing the organization requires four distinct decisions.

**Structure.** How is the team organized?

- *Territorial* — each rep owns a geographic territory. Simple, low overlap, but forces a single rep to cover multiple products and customer types.
- *Product* — each rep specializes in one product line. Deep product knowledge; risk of coverage gaps and customer confusion when one customer is served by multiple reps.
- *Customer* — each rep serves a specific customer type (enterprise, SMB, government). Deep customer-type knowledge; same product-coverage challenges as territorial.
- *Matrix* — combinations of the above. Most enterprise organizations. Complex to manage; necessary when territory, product, and customer dimensions all matter.

**Recruitment.** Sales managers look for specific traits: resilience, genuine curiosity, persuasive communication, coachability. Sources vary — recent graduates for high-volume roles (lower cost, trainable), experienced professionals for complex enterprise sales (faster ramp, higher cost).

**Training.** Product knowledge, selling process, customer-specific knowledge. New reps often don't know the product well enough to sell it convincingly in the first few months — training duration is a direct investment in ramp time.

**Compensation.** Base salary plus commission, bonuses, and contests. Structure shapes behavior: reps optimize for what they're paid on. A commission structure based on revenue drives revenue-maximizing behavior, which may or may not align with the firm's gross-margin objectives. A structure based on gross margin aligns rep incentives with firm profitability. A structure based on new logo acquisition drives new business but may underinvest in renewals. Every compensation design produces the behaviors it rewards; the design question is whether those behaviors are the right ones.

**Evaluation.** Revenue, gross margin, win rate, pipeline velocity, customer retention. The evaluation framework should match the compensation structure — measuring reps on metrics they're not compensated for produces confusing signals.

## Sales Promotion: What It Does, and What It Doesn't

Sales promotion is short-term incentives for purchase. Direct call to action. It moves goods quickly, drives trial, and accelerates decisions. It is not a brand-building tool.

The distinction matters because many firms use sales promotion as a substitute for the harder work of building brand equity. A brand that runs promotions continuously trains customers to wait for the next deal. The discount becomes the expected price. Full-price sales erode. The brand's ability to charge a premium collapses. Sales promotion done right is tactical and bounded; done wrong, it's a brand-equity slow leak.

Sales promotions split into two categories based on who they target.

**Consumer-oriented promotions** pull demand directly from end customers:

*Coupons* reduce price; prompt immediate purchase. HelloFresh mails coupons offering 16 free meals to drive subscription signups. The math: they're willing to take the acquisition loss on the coupon if the customer's lifetime value exceeds it. The bet only works if the product is good enough that a customer who starts doesn't immediately cancel.

*Samples* offer free product trial. Most effective in the introduction stage — the customer who can't evaluate the product without experiencing it needs a risk-free first encounter. Food, beauty, and software (free trials) all use samples heavily.

*Contests and sweepstakes* generate engagement and social sharing. They drive conversation but don't always drive purchase; the best ones create purchase occasions as the entry mechanism.

*BOGO (buy one get one)* drives volume, useful for clearing inventory or protecting shelf space during a competitor's promotional push.

*Loyalty programs* reward repeat purchase over time. The Hilton Honors Silver/Gold/Diamond structure — reward nights, tier benefits — is a loyalty program. The key design question: does the program reward the behavior the firm actually wants (repeated full-price purchases, relationship depth) or does it simply reward spending volume at any price?

McDonald's "free fries on Friday when you purchase a menu item through the app" combines sales promotion with app adoption: the promotion drives both Friday traffic and a behavioral habit (ordering through the app rather than at the counter) that reduces friction for future purchases.

**Trade-oriented promotions** push demand through the distribution channel:

*Push allowances* are discounts to retailers for stocking the product. The retailer captures the margin; the manufacturer captures distribution.

*Cooperative advertising* splits advertising costs between manufacturer and retailer. The manufacturer gets distribution support; the retailer gets partially-funded marketing.

*Trade shows and dealer incentives* are conferences and contests for distributors — relationship-building in the channel that precedes end-customer demand.

*In-store displays* are point-of-purchase materials that the manufacturer provides to the retailer for positioning at the shelf. The display does the last-meter selling.

## The Ethical Boundaries

Personal selling and sales promotion both generate specific ethical risks. Naming them explicitly is more useful than a general admonition to "behave ethically."

**In personal selling**, the structural risks are predictable. Salespeople work independently, often with expense accounts and discretionary closing tools. Without controls, the common failure modes are: expense account abuse (padding, personal use), sales data inflation (overstating pipeline under quota pressure), bribes and kickbacks in both directions (a rep paying a buyer for favorable treatment, or a buyer offering kickbacks to a rep), dishonest product claims (overstating capabilities, hiding limitations), and competitor slander (negative claims that could constitute defamation or false advertising). The prevention is structural: clear codes of conduct, pipeline data integrity checks, supervised training, and a management culture that doesn't pressure reps into unethical behavior by making unattainable quotas the only path to income.

**In sales promotion**, the specific risks are different: misleading promotion design (the "buy one get one free" where the firm raises the price of one item just before the promotion to make the deal illusory), promotion fraud (customers gaming single-use coupons or rebates through fake accounts), and bait-and-switch (advertising one product to draw customers in, then aggressively steering them toward a different one). All three are illegal under various state and federal statutes; all three also reduce $B$ permanently when customers realize what happened.

The connection to the value ratio is the same as every other chapter: trust is a component of $B$. A short-term promotion that misleads customers may produce one quarter's revenue and several years of reduced willingness to believe the brand's next claim.

## What the Chapter Leaves You With

Personal selling and sales promotion sit at opposite ends of one dimension: depth versus breadth. Personal selling is the highest-depth, lowest-breadth tool in the promotion mix. A sales rep builds a relationship one customer at a time — expensive, slow, powerful for complex high-value purchases. Sales promotion is high-breadth, low-depth — it reaches many customers quickly with a simple stimulus, ideal for accelerating trial or clearing inventory, dangerous if overused.

Both work. Neither replaces the other. The Hilton Grand Vacations model uses both deliberately: the promotion produces volume, the salesperson produces commitment.

The discipline is knowing which job each is doing — and resisting the temptation to ask either one to do the other's job. A firm that substitutes sales promotion for brand building is spending on short-term volume at the cost of long-term equity. A firm that substitutes advertising for personal selling in complex B2B sales is spending reach to do a job that requires depth.
