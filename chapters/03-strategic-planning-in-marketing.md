# Chapter 3 — Strategic Planning in Marketing

*Strategy is the discipline of pointing the levers at something specific. Most plans fail not because the levers are wrong, but because they were never pointed.*

---

Here is a puzzle worth sitting with before we talk about strategy.

Frito-Lay — the producer of Doritos, Lay's, and Cheetos — is a $13 billion business. Every few months, a new flavor appears on the shelf. Dill pickle. Chesapeake Bay Crab Spice. Chile Limón. The natural assumption is that food scientists in a Frito-Lay lab run experiments, find the winners, and ship them.

That assumption is wrong in an instructive way.

Frito-Lay crowdsources many of its new flavors through a contest called "Do Us a Flavor." Customers submit ideas, the company shortlists three finalists, and the winners earn cash prizes and shelf space. A related campaign called "Turn Up the Flavor" extended this: three limited-time flavors inspired by music genres — hip-hop, pop, rock — paired with a partnership with singer-songwriter Bebe Rexha, who wrote a theme song. Unique codes on chip packets unlocked her new tracks.

Ask the strategic question: why would a $13 billion brand outsource a core product decision to its own customers?

The answer isn't that Frito-Lay's R&D lab is incompetent. It isn't. The answer is that in the snack-food category — mature market, thin margins, well-defended shelf space — the rate-limiting factor on growth is not flavor quality. It is *customer engagement*. A flavor developed in a lab competes on taste. A flavor developed by a customer competes on taste *and* on the social and psychological value of seeing your own idea on a real bag in a real store. The contest doesn't just produce flavors; it produces a story the customer tells about themselves. That story is a marketing asset no amount of internal R&D could buy.

This is what strategy looks like when it's working: a specific choice that responds to a specific market reality and produces a specific outcome that competitors who chose differently cannot easily match. There is no magic in it. But there is a discipline — a sequence of commitments, each one more concrete than the last — and most firms skip most of it.

## Strategy Is a Sequence of Choices, Not a List of Activities

Chapter 2 gave you the value ratio and four levers. This chapter asks the question Chapter 2 didn't answer: *where do you point them?*

The answer is strategy. But strategy is a word used so often for so many things that it has nearly lost its meaning. A vision statement is called strategy. A marketing calendar is called strategy. A slide deck about brand positioning is called strategy. None of those things are strategy. They are artifacts — the residue left over after strategy has been done, or documents produced in its absence.

Here is a definition precise enough to be useful: strategy is a sequence of choices about where to compete, with whom, and how. What makes it a sequence — and not a list — is that each layer constrains the next. If the vision is "to be earth's most customer-centric company," then the product decision must be customer-centric, the pricing decision must be customer-centric, and the executive who wants to optimize for warehouse efficiency instead of the customer has to argue explicitly against the vision. That friction is not a bug. It is the mechanism by which the vision does any work at all.

A vision that constrains decisions is uncomfortable. A vision that doesn't constrain decisions is comfortable, and useless.

The strategic planning process runs through five steps, each more concrete than the last.

**Vision** — where does the firm want to be? Amazon's vision statement: *"To be earth's most customer-centric company; to build a place where people can come to find and discover anything they might want to buy online."* That's doing real work. Compare Fujitsu's: *"Understanding you better — serving you best."* That could be any firm that has heard of customers. It constrains nothing.

**Mission** — why does the firm exist, and how does it serve customers? The mission is more operational than the vision; it answers what the firm does, for whom, and how it's differentiated. IKEA: *"To offer a wide range of well-designed, functional home furnishing products at prices so low that as many people as possible will be able to afford them."* That's a mission. It tells you what IKEA makes, who it's for, and what it's optimizing. It also tells you what IKEA is *not* doing — it's not making expensive furniture for a luxury market. Every word in that mission closes a door, and that's precisely what makes it valuable.

Theodore Levitt's 1960 essay "Marketing Myopia" is the canonical argument for customer-oriented missions over product-oriented ones. His observation: American railroads declined not because transportation declined, but because they defined themselves as railroad companies rather than transportation companies. When cars and planes emerged, they couldn't see themselves in that business. A firm that defines itself by its product is one new product away from irrelevance. A firm that defines itself by the customer need it solves can follow that need wherever it goes.

**Gap analysis** — where is the firm now, and what's stopping it from getting to the vision? The gap analysis is the diagnostic step. Current state: market share is growing 5 percent per year. Desired state: 25 percent growth over three years. Gap: a lower-priced competitor entered last year, advertising hasn't refreshed in 18 months, production costs rose but prices didn't. The gap analysis doesn't solve the problem. It names it precisely enough that the next step can.

**Objectives** — specific, time-bound targets for the next 12 months. This is where most strategy documents collapse. The goal "improve brand awareness" is not an objective. It is a wish. The objective "achieve 35 percent aided brand recall among 18–34 urban consumers in our top 5 markets by the end of Q4" is an objective. It is specific enough to fail. And that's the test: if you can't fail it, it's not constraining anything.

The acronym for this discipline is **SMART** — Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Realistic, Time-bound. The acronym is less important than the underlying logic: an objective that can't be failed can't be learned from. A firm that consistently writes unfailable objectives is manufacturing the appearance of progress without the reality.

**Monitor** — track progress against objectives, adjust when the environment shifts. This step closes the loop. Objectives without monitoring are a snapshot; objectives with monitoring are a control system.

<!-- → DIAGRAM: Strategic planning as a five-step chain. Vision (most abstract) → Mission → Gap Analysis → Objectives → Monitor. Arrows show each step constraining the next. The student should see strategy as a sequence of progressively concrete commitments, not a list of activities. -->

The trade-off at every layer is clarity versus flexibility. A specific objective is easy to fail; a vague one is impossible to fail and impossible to learn from. Firms that win sustainably err on the side of clarity. They commit, fail some commitments, and learn from the failures. Firms that err on the side of flexibility survive every year without ever winning.

## When You Have Multiple Products, You Have Multiple Strategies

Apple sells phones, tablets, laptops, watches, headphones, and services. Each operates in a different market, against different competitors, at a different stage of growth, with different margins. Apple's strategic decisions about the iPhone bear almost no resemblance to its decisions about the iPod Touch.

In a recent full fiscal year, the iPhone produced 52 percent of Apple's revenue — $192 billion in 2021 alone. The iPad held 31.5 percent of the global tablet market in Q1 2022. The MacBook held 15.3 percent of the laptop market. The Apple Watch led the smartwatch category in a segment growing 13 percent year-over-year. And the iPod Touch was discontinued in May 2022, with remaining inventory sold through.

If you applied the same strategy to all five products — same investment, same growth target, same marketing mix — you would over-invest in the iPod (which the company is exiting) and under-invest in the iPhone (which is funding the rest of the company). Different products need different strategies. The portfolio question is: how does a firm decide which products to invest in, which to milk, which to question, and which to retire?

The Boston Consulting Group developed a 2×2 matrix that classifies products on two axes: market growth rate (high or low) and the firm's market share within the category (high or low). Four quadrants follow.

**Stars** — high share, high growth. Apple's iPhone. Invest aggressively; these are the firm's future and typically its present.

**Cash cows** — high share, low growth. Apple's iPad and MacBook on the source material's reading. They generate cash to fund stars and question marks and require minimal new investment to maintain. You don't grow cash cows; you harvest them.

**Question marks** — low share, high growth. Apple's Watch fits here: the smartwatch market was growing fast, but Apple's share of the total wearables category was still contested. The strategic question is whether to invest more — converting the question mark to a star — or divest. Both answers are defensible. Deferring the decision is not.

**Dogs** — low share, low growth. Apple's iPod. The market had matured and contracted; Apple's share within it was irrelevant to overall company performance. The prescription is to divest, harvest, or kill.

<!-- → DIAGRAM: BCG matrix as a 2×2 with the four quadrants labeled (Stars, Cash Cows, Question Marks, Dogs). Each quadrant gets one Apple product as an example: iPhone (Star), iPad and MacBook (Cash Cows), Apple Watch (Question Mark), iPod (Dog). The student should see the framework's classification logic before reading the explanation. -->

The matrix forces a deliberate decision about each product line based on share and growth, rather than on attachment or inertia. That forcing function is more valuable than any specific recommendation the matrix produces. A firm that uses BCG to have a real conversation about its dog products — even if it concludes that the dog is worth keeping for strategic reasons — is doing something most firms don't do.

The matrix also has real limits, and ignoring them is expensive. It collapses two continuous variables into four boxes; a product on the line between cash cow and dog is treated identically to one safely inside the dog quadrant even when the strategic implications differ. It describes the current state, not the future: a star today can be a dog in five years if the technology is disrupted. And it misses strategic interdependencies — a dog with a small, loyal customer base might be keeping a relationship that buys other products at much higher margins.

A second 2×2 — the SWOT analysis — looks at the firm itself rather than its portfolio. **Strengths** and **weaknesses** are internal; **opportunities** and **threats** are external. Starbucks, as an example: strengths include strong brand equity and international supply chain; weaknesses include high prices relative to competition and product imitability; opportunities include expansion in developing markets and subscription coffee services; threats include competition from lower-cost sellers and inflation's effect on discretionary spending.

<!-- → TABLE: SWOT 2×2 for Starbucks. Quadrants: Strengths (top-left), Weaknesses (top-right), Opportunities (bottom-left), Threats (bottom-right). Source factors as bullets in each. The student should see the asymmetry: a SWOT done well prioritizes within each quadrant; a SWOT done badly is a brainstorm with no ranking. -->

The most common failure mode in SWOT is treating it as a brainstorm rather than a decision tool. A SWOT that lists ten things in each quadrant with no ranking hasn't done the work. A useful SWOT prioritizes within each quadrant — which strengths are most defensible, which weaknesses are most exposed — and then pairs strengths against opportunities (where can we win?) and weaknesses against threats (where are we exposed?). A SWOT that ends without those pairings is a list, not an analysis.

## Four Ways to Grow

Once a firm has classified its products and assessed its situation, it picks a growth direction. There are four canonical options.

**Market penetration** — grow share in existing markets with existing products. Procter & Gamble spent $4.7 billion on advertising in 2020 to defend and grow share in categories like detergent and personal care. The strategy is expensive and slow, and the reason firms keep doing it is that the alternative — ceding share — is also expensive and, eventually, unrecoverable.

**Product development** — sell new or improved products to existing markets. Tide laundry detergent has had over 50 formulation changes in 40 years, each marketed as "new and improved." The brand stays; the product evolves. The customer doesn't have to be persuaded to trust a new brand; they just have to be persuaded to try a new version of one they already use. Auto manufacturers run the same playbook on a longer cycle.

**Market development** — sell existing products to new markets. Facebook started as a platform for Harvard undergraduates, then expanded to other universities, then to the public, then internationally. The product didn't change in any fundamental way at each step; the market expanded. Facebook now has nearly 3 billion users. Each step was a market-development decision, not a product decision.

**Product diversification** — sell new products to new markets. The riskiest strategy because both variables shift simultaneously: new product the firm has never made before, sold to customers the firm has never sold to before. General Electric started as a lighting business and now makes medical devices, household appliances, aircraft engines, and financial services. Three sub-types: *concentric* (new products related to the existing line), *horizontal* (new products unrelated to the existing line, sold to the same customers), and *conglomerate* (new products to entirely new markets).

The four-strategy frame is a forcing question for any growth initiative: which of these four are we doing? If a firm cannot answer that question for a given project, it is probably expanding without strategic intent, which is more expensive than choosing.

## The Marketing Plan and the Discipline of Measurement

Imagine a firm reports 10 percent sales growth. The dashboard glows green.

Now imagine you learn the category grew 20 percent and the largest competitor grew 25 percent. The same 10 percent reads entirely differently. The firm didn't grow. It lost share while revenue numbers moved in a direction that could be confused with growing.

This is one of the central failures of strategic measurement: the number you reach for first is rarely the number that matters most. The discipline is choosing — deliberately and in advance — the right numbers to track, and then tracking them against the right benchmarks.

A **marketing plan** is the document that translates strategy into specific actions. It has eight elements that do most of the work: an executive summary, the mission statement, a SWOT, SMART objectives with named owners, target segments and buyer personas, the four-P strategy, action programs specifying what gets done by whom by when at what cost, and a budget with controls. A plan that has the first six but not the last two is a marketing essay. A plan with all eight is a plan.

<!-- → TABLE: Marketing plan anatomy — two columns: Section, Purpose. Eight rows, one per element. Rows 7 and 8 (Action Programs and Budget/Controls) highlighted or bolded to signal that these are the two most commonly omitted. Caption: "A plan that stops at row 6 is a marketing essay. Rows 7 and 8 are what make it executable." -->

The measurement question lives in the last element. **Marketing metrics** are anything you can measure. **Key performance indicators** are the subset tied directly to a business objective, with a target and a time frame. Every KPI is a metric. Most metrics are not KPIs.

Revenue growth is the simplest KPI:

$$\text{Revenue Growth \%} = \left( \frac{\text{This Year's Revenue}}{\text{Last Year's Revenue}} - 1 \right) \times 100$$

If 2022 revenue is $1.8 billion and 2021 revenue is $1.4 billion:

$$\frac{1.8}{1.4} - 1 = 0.2857 \times 100 = 28.57\%$$

By itself, 28.57 percent is meaningless without context. The KPI to pair it with is **relative market share** — the firm's share divided by the leading competitor's share. If the firm holds 5 percent and the leader holds 8 percent, relative market share is $5\% \div 8\% = 0.625$. Below 1.0 means you are not the leader; how far below tells you how much ground you have to cover.

A more forward-looking measure is **Customer Lifetime Value (CLV)** — the estimated profit a customer will generate over their entire relationship with the firm:

$$\text{CLV} = (\text{Annual Profit per Customer} \times \text{Average Years Retained}) - \text{Customer Acquisition Cost}$$

Run this across four hypothetical segments:

| Segment | Annual Profit | Years Retained | CAC | CLV |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Millennial parents | $10,000 | 12 | $750 | **$119,250** |
| Millennial singles | $18,000 | 12 | $750 | **$215,250** |
| Working moms | $22,000 | 8 | $1,200 | **$174,800** |
| Gen Z emerging professionals | $34,000 | 2 | $1,600 | **$66,400** |

If the goal is to maximize CLV, the priority order is: Millennial singles first ($215,250), Working moms second ($174,800), Millennial parents third ($119,250), Gen Z last ($66,400). Gen Z has the highest annual profit per customer — $34,000 — but the worst CLV because they are retained for only two years.

Now ask: what changes that conclusion?

Suppose the customer experience team can extend Gen Z retention from 2 years to 8 years:

$$\text{CLV}_{\text{Gen Z (revised)}} = (\$34{,}000 \times 8) - \$1{,}600 = \$270{,}400$$

Gen Z now has the highest CLV in the portfolio. The 6-year retention extension is worth $204,000 per customer. A firm that tracked only annual profit per customer would invest in Gen Z acquisition. A firm that tracked CLV would notice that retention — not acquisition — is the lever that matters most for that segment.

This is the discipline of measurement applied strategically: the same data, read through the right formula, produces a different decision.

<!-- → CHART: Bar chart comparing CLV across the four segments at default retention rates. A second bar overlay shows the Gen Z scenario with retention extended to 8 years. The student should see how a single retention number rewrites the whole prioritization. -->

The KPI problem has two failure modes that look opposite but produce the same outcome. Too few KPIs hides problems — a firm tracking only revenue can be losing share, churning customers, and burning loyalty without noticing. Too many KPIs also hides problems — a dashboard with 200 indicators is a spreadsheet no one reads, and the five indicators that actually matter get buried in the noise. The discipline is choosing five to ten, tying each to a specific objective, and ignoring the rest until they become relevant.

## Strategy Without Ethics Is Incomplete Strategy

The chapter's frameworks are value-neutral in the sense that they work regardless of what the firm is trying to accomplish. A SWOT analysis doesn't know whether you are analyzing a hospital or a cigarette company. A BCG matrix doesn't know whether the cash cows are selling food or opioids. The frameworks are tools, and tools don't have opinions.

But strategy without ethics is also incomplete strategy, and for a reason that doesn't require invoking ethics at all.

Toyota's unintended-acceleration scandal during the 2000s is the instructive case. Toyota vehicles experienced acceleration problems that generated over 6,200 complaints to the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration and were linked to more than 89 deaths over five years. Toyota initially blamed driver error, then issued recalls for floor mats, and ultimately concealed a flawed gas-pedal design while misrepresenting the situation to regulators and Congress. The Department of Justice imposed a $1.2 billion criminal fine — the largest in US automotive history — after finding that Toyota's effort to protect its corporate image directly led to preventable deaths.

The strategic mistake at Toyota was not insufficient marketing spend. It was prioritizing brand image over customer safety — a strategic choice, made implicitly, never written into a vision statement, but consistently honored in operational decisions. The values embedded in a firm's actual decisions are its real strategy, regardless of what the mission statement says.

The customer context is also shifting in ways that make ethics increasingly a strategic variable, not just a moral one. Millennials (born 1981–1996) and Gen Z (born 1997–2012) make up roughly 65 percent of the world's population. A First Insight report found that 62 percent of Millennials and Gen Z are willing to pay more for sustainable products, compared to 54 percent of Gen X and 39 percent of Boomers. A Nielsen survey of 30,000+ consumers across 60 countries found 66 percent of global consumers willing to pay more for sustainable products, and 73 percent of Millennials specifically.

What this means for the value ratio from Chapter 2: younger customers' $B$ — the numerator in $V = B/P$ — now includes a measurable sustainability and ethics component. A strategic plan that ignores that variable is not a complete plan for a firm whose primary customers are Millennials or Gen Z.

Fortune Global 500 firms now spend approximately $20 billion per year on corporate social responsibility initiatives. The reason is not purely altruistic. The math of $V = B/P$ has shifted, and firms that read that shift are responding to it strategically.

## Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 2 ended with the firm holding the value ratio and the four levers. This chapter asked: where do you point them?

The answer is a sequence: vision constrains mission, mission shapes the gap analysis, the gap analysis generates objectives, objectives govern the marketing plan, the plan produces actions, and KPIs measure whether the actions are working. At each layer, the discipline is the same — be specific enough to be wrong, so that you can learn from what goes wrong.

The portfolio tools (BCG, SWOT) help a multi-product firm avoid the mistake of applying the same strategy to every product regardless of where it is in its lifecycle. The four growth strategies (penetration, product development, market development, diversification) name what kind of growth the firm is after. The marketing plan turns those choices into specific action programs with budgets and owners.

What you can now do that you could not before: take any organization's strategic documents — vision, mission, SWOT, marketing plan, KPI dashboard — and tell, in five to ten minutes of careful reading, whether the strategy is real or theatre. A vision that could belong to any firm in the industry is theatre. A SWOT that lists everything without ranking is theatre. A KPI dashboard that no one changes behavior to hit is theatre. A plan with brilliant diagnosis and no action programs is theatre.

Strategy done well is just a series of choices specific enough that the firm has to live with them. That's all it is. The difficulty is not in understanding what that means. The difficulty is in being willing to close the doors that specific choices close.

### Warm-Up

**1. The SMART test.** For each objective below, identify which SMART criterion it fails most badly, then rewrite it to pass all five:

- "Grow our social media presence this year."
- "Increase revenue in the enterprise segment."
- "Become the most trusted brand in our category by Q3."
- "Reduce customer churn."

*(Tests: ability to apply SMART as a diagnostic, not just recite the acronym. Difficulty: Low.)*

**2. Name the growth strategy.** A mid-size athletic apparel brand is considering four initiatives. For each, name which of the four growth strategies it represents (market penetration, product development, market development, product diversification) and explain in one sentence why:

- Launching a children's clothing line sold to the same retail partners.
- Running a loyalty-points program to increase repeat purchases from existing customers.
- Entering the Brazilian market with the existing product line.
- Acquiring a sports nutrition company and selling protein bars under the apparel brand.

*(Tests: recognition of strategy types in slightly disguised form. Difficulty: Low.)*

**3. BCG placement.** A firm sells four products. Annual revenue and estimated market growth rates are: Product A — $420M, 2% market growth; Product B — $85M, 18% market growth; Product C — $310M, 1% market growth; Product D — $40M, 22% market growth. The firm is the market leader in A and C, holds roughly 8% share in B (leader holds 30%), and holds 6% share in D (leader holds 25%).

Place each product in the BCG matrix. For each, name the prescribed strategic action. *(Tests: ability to apply the classification logic, not just name the quadrants. Difficulty: Low-medium.)*

### Application

**4. Compute and interpret the KPIs.** A firm reports the following data: revenue this year $2.3 billion, revenue last year $1.9 billion; total category size $38 billion; leading competitor's revenue $5.1 billion.

Compute: year-over-year revenue growth; the firm's market share; the leading competitor's market share; relative market share (firm's share ÷ leader's share).

Then write one paragraph — no more than 150 words — interpreting the firm's strategic position. What does the combination of these four numbers tell you that any one of them alone would not? *(Tests: paired-metric discipline; ability to read a strategic situation from numbers rather than anecdote. Difficulty: Medium.)*

**5. CLV segment decision.** Use the four-segment CLV table from the chapter. A product team proposes investing $300,000 to improve onboarding for Gen Z customers, with the goal of increasing their average retention from 2 years to 4 years.

Compute the revised Gen Z CLV. Then answer: does this investment change the segment priority ranking? By how much per customer does the ranking change? What retention length would Gen Z need to achieve to surpass Millennial singles as the highest-CLV segment? *(Tests: CLV arithmetic and the discipline of asking "what would have to be true" before committing to a strategic bet. Difficulty: Medium.)*

**6. Read a real SWOT.** Find a published SWOT analysis for any real firm — a case study, an investor presentation, an MBA report, or a news article that frames things this way. Identify two problems with how it was constructed. Common failure modes: items misclassified as internal when they're external (or vice versa); no prioritization within quadrants; vague items that wouldn't drive any decision ("we have talented employees").

Rewrite one quadrant to fix the problems you found. *(Tests: SWOT as a decision tool rather than a worksheet. Difficulty: Medium.)*

### Synthesis

**7. Strategy chain stress test.** Pick a firm you know well enough to have an opinion about — a company you've worked for, a brand you follow closely, or a business from a previous case study. Write:

- One vision statement (specific enough to constrain at least one type of decision)
- One mission statement (specific enough to say what the firm won't do)
- Three SMART objectives for the coming year
- One growth strategy (which of the four?) with a one-paragraph justification

Then check the chain: does the growth strategy follow from the objectives? Do the objectives follow from the mission? Would a competitor reading the mission know something the firm is *not* going to do?

If the chain breaks anywhere, fix it. Note where the break was and what fixing it required. *(Tests: constraint propagation across the strategy chain. Difficulty: Medium-high.)*

**8. The measurement trap.** A firm's marketing team sets the following KPI for the year: "increase website traffic by 40 percent." By December, traffic is up 43 percent. The team declares success.

But the sales team reports that conversion rates dropped from 3.2 percent to 1.8 percent. Revenue is flat.

Using the frameworks in this chapter, explain what went wrong at the level of KPI design. What metric was the team optimizing for instead of the outcome they actually wanted? Redesign the measurement plan: propose three KPIs that, together, would have caught this problem before December. For each KPI, name the gaming risk — the way a team could hit the number without producing the underlying outcome. *(Tests: the distinction between a metric and a KPI; the anti-gaming discipline of leading + lagging indicator pairing. Difficulty: Medium-high.)*

### Challenge

**9. Strategy theatre autopsy.** Find the public strategic language of a firm you believe is doing strategy theatre — a mission statement, an annual report summary, or a "our strategy" section from a corporate website. Copy three specific phrases.

For each phrase: explain what decision the phrase was supposed to constrain, then describe what the firm actually did in the relevant domain. If the phrase failed to constrain any decision, rewrite it in one sentence so that it would.

The win condition is not identifying that the language is vague — that's easy. The win condition is being specific about *what choice was being avoided* and *what would happen to the firm if it made that choice explicit*. *(Tests: reading strategy critically rather than reverently. Difficulty: High.)*
