## Core mechanisms

**The buying center (DMU).** Six roles — initiator, influencer (business + technical), gatekeeper, buyer, decider, user — that participate in B2B purchase decisions. The same person can occupy multiple roles. Not all roles appear in every decision. Marketing implication: map the buying center per prospect; concentrate on 1–2 dominant roles per deal.

**The 8-stage B2B buying process:** problem recognition → need description → product specification → supplier search → proposal solicitation → supplier selection → order-routine specification → performance review. Buy class determines which stages activate fully.

**Derived demand.** Demand for B2B goods exists *because of* downstream consumer demand, not directly. A B2B firm whose product has derived demand is tied to a B2C market it doesn't sell to. Knowing which B2C demand drives your B2B demand is core to forecasting and strategy.

## Vocabularies

**Six structural differences between B2B and B2C:**
- Decision nature (self/household vs. organization)
- Buyer count, purchase size (many small vs. fewer large)
- Demand type (direct vs. derived)
- Buying cycle (short vs. long)
- Marketing reliance (mass marketing vs. personal selling)
- Geographic concentration (dispersed vs. industry-clustered)

**Four types of B2B buyers:**
- **Producers** — buy to transform into other goods (Emerson, Burger King, OSI/Chipotle/McDonald's)
- **Resellers** — retailers (Walmart, Target), wholesalers, brokers
- **Government** — US government is largest single buyer in the world; SAM registration + GSA
- **Institutions** — nonprofits, charities, churches, hospitals, schools

**Four buy classes:**
- Straight rebuy (routine, familiar product, familiar supplier)
- Modified rebuy (similar with changes — specs, supplier, terms)
- New-task buy (first-time purchase, most complex)
- Systems selling / solution sale (complete bundle from one supplier)

**Five categories of B2B influence (parallel to but distinct from Ch 5's consumer categories):**
- External — economic, political/legal, competition, social environment
- Internal — organizational objectives, technology compatibility, workforce skills
- Individual — age, education, position, personality
- Interpersonal — collective dynamics, expertise hierarchy, organizational politics
- Conditional — financial condition, availability

## Named trade-offs (the MKBHD move)

- **Relationship economics vs. mass-marketing economics.** Each game has its own scaling logic; mixing them produces wasted spend. B2B leans relationship; B2C leans mass.
- **Buying-center role focus.** Talk to one role and you miss five; talk to all six and you spread too thin. Discipline = identify the 1–2 dominant roles per deal.
- **Process completeness vs. timing reality.** Eight stages produce best decisions but take longest. Buyers compress under deadline; vendors should adapt but document what's being skipped.

## Ethical / legal frameworks

- **Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA, 1977)** — prohibits bribes to foreign officials; applies anywhere in the world; covers public companies + officers, directors, employees, stockholders, agents; enforced jointly by SEC + DOJ.
- **Federal Trade Commission (FTC) price-fixing prohibition** — covers price agreements, discount agreements, capacity/quota agreements among competitors.
- **Lanham Act** (referenced from Ch 2) — false advertising claims; competitors can sue.
