## Core mechanism

**Strategy as a chain of progressively concrete choices.** The five-step strategic planning chain: Vision → Mission → Gap Analysis → Objectives → Monitor. Each step constrains the next. A strategy where vision doesn't constrain mission doesn't constrain objectives is a strategy with broken links — and broken links produce strategy theatre.

## Vocabularies

**Three levels of strategy:**
- Corporate-level — across the entire organization (multi-SBU firms)
- Business-level — within a single business unit or product line
- Functional — how individual functions (marketing, HR, ops) execute

**Vision vs. mission:**
- Vision — *where* the firm is going (forward-looking, aspirational)
- Mission — *why* the firm exists and *how* it serves customers (operational, present-tense)
- Mission flavors: customer-oriented (defines firm by need solved) vs. product-oriented (defines firm by what it makes); customer-oriented is generally more durable

**SMART criteria for objectives:**
- **S**pecific
- **M**easurable
- **A**ttainable
- **R**ealistic
- **T**ime-bound

**Goals vs. objectives:**
- Goals — broad, long-term outcomes (3–5 years)
- Objectives — specific short-term action targets (≤12 months)

**The four growth strategies (Ansoff-style matrix):**
- Market penetration — existing products, existing markets
- Product development — new products, existing markets
- Market development — existing products, new markets
- Product diversification — new products, new markets (highest risk)
  - Concentric — related to existing line
  - Horizontal — unrelated products to existing customers
  - Conglomerate — unrelated products to new markets

## Frames

**BCG matrix (2×2: market share × market growth):**
- Stars (high share, high growth) — invest aggressively
- Cash cows (high share, low growth) — milk for cash to fund stars/question marks
- Question marks (low share, high growth) — invest more or divest; don't defer
- Dogs (low share, low growth) — divest, harvest, or kill

**SWOT (2×2: internal/external × positive/negative):**
- Strengths — internal positives
- Weaknesses — internal negatives
- Opportunities — external positives
- Threats — external negatives
- Discipline rule: prioritize within each quadrant; pair S×O and W×T

**Marketing plan structure (compressed to 8 elements from OpenStax 12):**
1. Executive summary
2. Mission statement
3. SWOT analysis
4. Objectives (SMART)
5. Segmentation, target market, buyer personas, positioning
6. Marketing strategy (4Ps decisions)
7. Action programs (what / who / when / cost)
8. Budget and controls

**Marketing metrics vs. KPIs:** Every KPI is a metric; not every metric is a KPI. KPIs are the subset tied to specific business objectives with targets and time frames.

**Three KPI categories:**
- Business objective — total sales/revenue, revenue growth, profitability (gross margin)
- Sales/revenue generation — Average Revenue per Customer (ARPC), Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC), Customer Retention Rate
- Market share — category share, relative market share (= firm's share / leading competitor's share)
- Customer support — Customer Satisfaction Score (CSAT), Customer Resolution Rate, Customer Resolution Time

## Equations

**Revenue growth:**
$$\text{Revenue Growth \%} = \left( \frac{\text{This Year}}{\text{Last Year}} - 1 \right) \times 100$$
Worked example in chapter: $1.8B / $1.4B = 28.57% growth.

**Relative market share:**
$$\text{Relative Market Share} = \frac{\text{Firm's Share}}{\text{Leading Competitor's Share}}$$
Worked example: 5% / 8% = 62.5%.

**Customer Satisfaction Score (CSAT):**
$$\text{CSAT} = \frac{\text{Satisfied Responses (4 or 5)}}{\text{Total Responses}} \times 100$$
Worked example: 50.33% from (308 + 447) / 1,500.

**Customer Lifetime Value (CLV):**
$$\text{CLV} = (\text{Annual Profit per Customer} \times \text{Years Retained}) - \text{CAC}$$
Worked example across 4 segments in chapter — Millennial parents $119,250; Millennial singles $215,250 (highest at default); Working moms $174,800; Gen Z professionals $66,400 (rises to $270,400 if retention extends to 8 years).

## Named trade-offs (the MKBHD move — reusable as a structural unit)

- **Strategy clarity vs. flexibility.** Specific vision constrains decisions but locks out pivots. Vague vision allows pivots but produces drift. Firms that win sustainably err on specificity.
- **BCG: classification vs. gradient.** The matrix collapses two continuous variables into four boxes, losing information at the boundaries. The discipline of forcing a quadrant decision is more valuable than the specific recommendations.
- **KPI count: too few hides problems; too many obscures them.** Choose 5–10 deliberately.
