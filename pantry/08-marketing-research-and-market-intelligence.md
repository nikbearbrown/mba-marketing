## Core mechanisms

**Marketing research = the discipline of producing information good enough to act on.** Four parts: figure out what's needed; gather through deliberate methods; analyze for decision-relevant signals; communicate to decision-makers.

**Seven-step research process:** define problem → develop plan → select method → design sample → collect data → analyze → report. Each step has a standard practice and a common failure mode.

**Three Vs of big data:** Volume, Velocity, Variety. ~2.5 quintillion bytes/day. 96% of Fortune 1000 reports success with big data + AI; 99% pursuing programs (NewVantage Partners 2021).

## Vocabularies

**Three categories of marketing information:**
- External data — outside the organization (customer surveys, competitor sites, social media, news)
- Internal data — already in firm's databases (sales, promotional effectiveness, pricing, R&D)
- Competitive intelligence — specialized external focused on competitors

**Four research designs:**
- Exploratory (qualitative — focus groups, interviews, secondary data review; landscape understanding)
- Descriptive (quantitative — surveys; describe market situation)
- Experimental / causal (A/B testing; cause-effect relationships)
- Ethnographic (observation in natural environment; how people actually use products)

**Primary vs. secondary data** — collected for current project vs. existing for other purposes; trade-off is current-and-tailored-but-expensive vs. cheap-and-fast-but-may-be-outdated.

**Qualitative vs. quantitative data** — words/descriptions/themes vs. numbers/statistics.

**Four data collection methods:**
- Observation (unobtrusive + mechanical) — captures real behavior; tells *what* not *why*
- Interview (one-on-one) — depth, customization; small samples, expensive
- Focus group (8–12 people) — group synergy, more respondents per hour; dominant personalities risk
- Survey (mailed / phone / in-person / electronic) — standardized at scale; depth limited

**Sampling vocabulary:**
- Census — entire population (US Census Bureau)
- Sample — portion of population
- Sample frame — eligible population
- Sampling unit — each respondent
- Sample size — how many

**Probability vs. nonprobability sampling:**
- Probability — known selection probability for every member; supports statistical inference (simple random, stratified, cluster, systematic)
- Nonprobability — unknown probability; cheaper but limits generalization (convenience, volunteer, snowball)

## Named trade-offs (the MKBHD move)

- **Data abundance vs. signal extraction.** Big data inverts the historical problem; the constraint is now extraction, not collection. Discipline = define question before touching data.
- **Depth vs. scale.** Qualitative methods give depth (why); quantitative methods give scale (what's typical). Mixed methods resolve.
- **Cost vs. generalizability.** Probability sampling defends statistical conclusions but expensive; nonprobability cheap but conclusions limited.

## Recurring named cases

- **LEGO "Ready for Girls"** — 7,000 parents and children, 7 countries, October 2021 (International Day of the Girl); gender-stereotype data (83% / 17% on dress-up; 71% / 29% on coding).
- **Cat toy subscription example** — running case for the 7-step process.
- **US Census** — every 10 years, mailed first then in-person follow-up; canonical mailed survey.
- **Telephone Consumer Protection Act 1991** — no phone surveys before 8 AM or after 9 PM in recipient's time zone.
- **A/B testing** — canonical experimental research method.
- **Baking soda ethnographic observation** — multiple uses (deodorizer, toothpaste, polish, baking).
- **NewVantage Partners 2021 survey** — 96% Fortune 1000 success with big data + AI; 99% pursuing programs.

## Ethics frameworks

- **AMA Statement of Ethics** — overarching framework
- **Informed consent** — participants must know they're participating, understand purpose, consent
- **Data privacy** — secure storage, stated-purpose-only use, anonymization on report
- **GDPR** (EU) — General Data Protection Regulation
- **CCPA** (California) — California Consumer Privacy Act
- **Honest reporting** — reflect what data actually showed; report null and unfavorable results; avoid cherry-picking and p-hacking
